# Saudi Arabia

The *de facto* leader of [[OPEC]] and the traditional swing producer in the global oil market. In the April 2026 Hormuz-closure crisis, Saudi is a counterparty whose Nash-optimal strategy matters as much as Iran's.

## April 2026 position
Per [[HFIResearch]] in [[wctw-the-oil-market-breaking-point]] (26 March 2026):
- **~3.05mmbpd of production shut in.**
- **East-West pipeline maxed out at ~4mmbpd via Yanbu** (Red Sea terminal, bypassing Hormuz).
- Per [[PauloMacro]] in [[oil-and-retail]] (21 April 2026): **exports collapsed from ~7mmbpd baseline to ~4mmbpd** — consistent with HFI's shut-in math a month earlier.
- Combined with [[UAE]]'s ~1.6-1.8mmbpd shut-in, the GCC is running a "max exports via Yanbu and Fujairah indefinitely — *and not a drop more*" strategy.
- HFI adds: *"if the current Iranian regime is not toppled, [the GCC] will be held hostage to this situation time and time again in the future. This is unacceptable even if there's some framework for a 'toll fee' going forward."* — same conclusion as Paulo's budget-arithmetic argument, via the security calculus.

## The Big-Beautiful-Deal problem
[[PauloMacro]] argues Saudi's optimal strategy under any Iran-concession peace deal is to keep the shut-in barrels in the ground **indefinitely** rather than accept the deal's implication. Logic:
- Iranian control of Hormuz **replaces OPEC (Saudi-controlled) with Iran** as the world's oil price-setter.
- It **subordinates the GCC monarchies to Tehran**, with implied tribute.
- The US security umbrella becomes meaningless if Trump signs such a deal — so Saudi no longer owes Trump a lower oil price.
- **Arithmetic:** `4mmbpd × $150/bbl = $750mn/day` > `7mmbpd × $80/bbl = $560mn/day`. Shut-in pays more than capitulation.
- Budget response: "Saudi would slash budgets, Project 2030 would become Project 2050."
- Paulo: *"Not a hard decision actually."*

## Role in the Nash game
Saudi is now a second player with a locked-in Nash strategy: it cannot accept Iranian control of Hormuz under any deal terms. Combined with Iran's own Nash strategy (per [[NorthStar]] and [[NashEquilibrium]]), the game has *two* locked equilibria — neither of which exits the crisis through price-lowering supply. Reinforces rather than contradicts the wiki's core geopolitical read.
