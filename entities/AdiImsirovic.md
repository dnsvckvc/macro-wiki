# Adi Imsirovic

Lecturer at the **University of Oxford** (via the Oxford Institute for Energy Studies) and former head of oil trading — 35 years in the industry plus ~5 years of teaching/observing. Author of *The Rivers of Money: Social and Economic History of Modern Oil Trading* (2025) and multiple prior books on oil trading, the Brent crude complex, and crude price discovery. The wiki's **fifteenth author** and first dedicated **physical-market microstructure specialist**.

## Unique contributions to the wiki

### The [[BrentComplex]] as structured taxonomy
Imsirovic's [[oilgroundup-adi-imsirovic]] appearance is the wiki's canonical source for how the Brent complex actually works:
- **Dated Brent** — physical, 10-30 days forward loading.
- **Forward Brent** — the original contract type; how Brent trading started (pre-futures); still the instrument CFDs settle against.
- **Brent futures** (ICE) — financially settled; normally ~2 months forward.
- **CFDs (Contracts for Differences)** — swaps referencing dated vs. forward (not dated vs. futures).
- **EFP (Exchange of Futures for Physicals)** — the bridge between financial and physical; normally 4-5 cents/bbl; 14-20+ cents during this crisis.

### The $30 Dated-Brent-over-futures spread = panic, not dysfunction
- *"I'd never seen"* this level. 4+ standard deviations above any historical reading.
- But it reflects genuine physical scarcity (refinery-can't-afford-to-run-out), not a failure of price discovery.
- *"The markets are, to be honest, working as well as they can under these crazy conditions."*

### The [[BrickAndMortarTrade]] coinage
Imsirovic's original analogy: in a financial crisis, capital flees to bonds or real estate ("brick and mortar"); in an oil crisis, capital flees to *physical barrels*. Refineries pay whatever it takes because shutdown means weeks of lost throughput.

### The futures-curve-is-not-a-forecast pushback
- Rejects Kevin Hassett's and [[IEA]]'s Fatih Birol's implicit claims that the futures curve forecasts flat-price paths or that "paper and physical will converge." The curve encodes seasonal storage incentives; the front stays above the back when the crisis persists because the *whole curve shifts together* as time passes.

### The monetary-policy-volatility channel
Imsirovic's most load-bearing macro-framework contribution:
> *"Oil does not give you a dividend… the only thing you have is capital gains. Everything else is cost. So the higher the cost of storage, the higher cost of time value of money, the lower the oil price by definition."*
- Interest rates = cost of storage.
- Monetary-policy volatility is itself bearish for oil because it destabilizes the cost-of-carry side of the storage equation.
- Fills in the third leg of the [[OilPriceRecessionDebate]] triangle (supply shock, CB response, *plus* MP-volatility).

### Reconciling the post-war scenarios
Imsirovic's mental model for what happens if the war resolves:
- **Flat price collapses** — potentially to $50-$60 by end of summer.
- **Backwardation persists** — Fujairah damage, shipping bottlenecks, storage logistics all keep the front tight.
- *"The whole curve will just shift upwards or downwards. You will still have backwardation, but the whole curve will just shift."*

This is the framework that makes [[AdamRozencwajg]]'s "forward curve hasn't repriced" observation *locally rational*: paper is pricing eventual OPEC-spare-capacity return, not denying the physical crisis.

## Views on record

- **Theory-of-storage position:** contango is natural state, backwardation is "wrong way round" (literal etymology). [[IlyaBouchouev]] (friend) holds the Keynesian opposite view; Imsirovic acknowledges both as defensible.
- **Volatility as a first-class oil-price driver** — not a secondary effect.
- **Saudi / UAE operational response:** remarkable. East-West pipeline ramped to 7mmbpd, ADNOC pumping 1.7mmbpd via Fujairah in spite of fire damage. *"Saved a lot of people's bacon."*
- **Trump jawboning → asymmetric vol**: spikes up $20 a day are impossible; drops $20 a day on a tweet are routine. Speculators have been "blown out" and jobs lost. Structural impairment of futures market as risk-management tool.
- **Post-war scenario:** flat-price undershoot after war ends, probably $50-$60. Rory pushes back on the aggressiveness of that number.

## The calibration-disagreement note with [[RoryJohnston]]
Imsirovic thinks war-resolution collapses crude to $50-$60 by end of summer; Johnston pushes back: *"I would be surprised to see $50 crude now, given that even already we've lost 450 million barrels of Gulf supply that hasn't been produced. That feels like a lot to me."* They agree on mechanism (flat price falls, backwardation persists) and disagree on the magnitude of the collapse. Rory's side of the disagreement is reinforced by [[HFIResearch]]'s 1bn→1.98bn cumulative-loss curve in [[wctw-the-oil-market-breaking-point-is-here]].
