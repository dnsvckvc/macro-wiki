# Norman Angell (1872–1967)

British economist and Nobel Peace Prize laureate (1933). Author of **[[TheGreatIllusion]]** (1909), which argued that the economic integration of European powers had become so deep that the cost of war would be catastrophic to all sides — making a major European war economically irrational and therefore improbable.

History disagreed: World War I began five years later.

## Wiki relevance
Cited by [[MarkoPapic]] in [[my-buddy-marko-broke-the-market]] as a self-deprecating comparison: *"I'm the Norman Angell of our era."* Papic's *Geopolitical Alpha* framework ([[FadeGeopoliticalAngst]], [[GeopoliticalAlpha]]) has taught the market that geopolitical events almost never have lasting impact — the same kind of analytically-correct-but-empirically-falsified argument Angell made. [[KevinMuir]] uses the parallel to suggest Papic's success has made the market complacent in the way Angell's book made pre-WWI Britain complacent.

Papic's response leans into the parallel: *"When World War Three starts and nobody is positioned correctly, they'll blame my book the way they did with The Great Illusion."*

The Norman Angell reference is thus the wiki's canonical parallel for "a correct framework that becomes dangerous precisely because it is too widely adopted."
