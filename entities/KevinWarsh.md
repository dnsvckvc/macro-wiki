# Kevin Warsh

Former Federal Reserve governor, widely reported as [[DonaldTrump]]'s preferred pick to replace [[JeromePowell]] as Fed Chair when Powell's term ends in May 2026. Confirmation status uncertain as of March 2026.

## Relevance in the wiki
- [[PauloMacro]] in [[the-anatomy-of-a-crash]] flags Warsh as the unknown variable in the Fed transition: assuming Warsh is confirmed, it is not clear he can or will "save the day" in an inflationary recession / oil crisis. The multi-month window of institutional inertia between now and the handoff is itself a risk factor.
- [[MichaelHowell]] in [[risks-of-another-financial-crisis-part-2]] explicitly flags Warsh's proposal to **reduce the size of the Fed's balance sheet** as *"increasingly risky"* given the Treasury market's structural fragility and the need for permanent liquidity backstops. Howell argues the system needs a *larger* Fed balance sheet (permanent standing repo facility), not a smaller one — making Warsh's appointment a potential accelerant of the crisis if he pursues balance-sheet reduction.
- Per [[LynAlden]] in [[macrovoices-525-lyn-alden]], Warsh's confirmation is **"on hold pending criminal investigation of Jay Powell"** — a striking delay factor. Alden partially pushes back on the alarmist framing: FOMC has 12 voters, not a dictatorship; Warsh's balance-sheet proposals are "largely liquidity-neutral"; and the war has narrowed the Warsh-vs-Powell difference because no credible Fed chair can cut into rising oil inflation anyway.
- Per [[SimonWhite]] in [[macrovoices-524-simon-white]], Warsh is likely to be **Arthur Burns, not Paul Volcker**. Burns was the Fed chair during Yom Kippur / OPEC 1 — dovish, didn't believe central banks could do much about supply-shock inflation, got staffers to invent *core inflation* so he could argue the headline problem wasn't really a problem. White: "Nobody is really sure he's going to be an inflation fighter. Quite the opposite." Consequence: the yield curve will likely **steepen** (OPEC 1 / Burns style) rather than flatten (OPEC 2 / Volcker style).

## The Stealth Accord constraint (per [[treasury-qe-warsh-fed-eclipsed]])

[[MichaelHowell]]'s 25 April 2026 piece sharpens the wiki's prior "increasingly risky" framing of Warsh's balance-sheet-shrinkage proposal to **structurally impossible**:

> *"Drastically shrinking the Fed's balance sheet, as mooted, and nostalgically returning to a 'scarce reserves' regime is no longer possible. This is because the system's linchpin has moved from reserves to dealer balance sheet capacity and the MOVE index. Consequently, the Fed is no longer the sole or even primary driver of liquidity. The US Treasury is."*

By the time Warsh arrives, the [[StealthAccord]] has already eclipsed the Fed:
- The US Treasury is in charge of growth (via [[TreasuryQE]] — bill issuance + vol-targeting buybacks).
- The Fed manages financial-market side-effects.
- Setting Fed funds is *"a pantomime, a sideshow."*
- Warsh's real test: managing a regime where a volatility shock could reverse everything overnight (collateral fragility per [[CollateralMultiplier]]).

Warsh-as-Volcker is a category error — even an aggressively hawkish Warsh cutting the Fed balance sheet would only make Treasury QE *more* relatively important, not less.
