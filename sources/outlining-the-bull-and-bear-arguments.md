## Summary
[[KevinMuir]]'s 20 April 2026 note — **part 2 of 2**, companion to [[making-sense-of-the-last-two-months]]. Muir systematically argues **both sides** of the April 2026 market before landing: a six-point bull case, a six-point bear case, and a candid conclusion. Importantly, this is Muir's first post that partially **walks back** his earlier conviction — he acknowledges the bull case is stronger than he initially thought, admits stocks may have "lost their discounting mechanism," and admits the "chase higher has been nothing short of shocking." But he explicitly reaffirms his **"short US tech / long rest of the world ex-US" trade**, calling the war-era action "emotional noise, muddling the issue." The piece also (a) introduces the wiki's first [[MarkoPapic]] "fade geopolitical angst" framework; (b) gives us the Trump [[OneBigBeautifulBill]] fiscal framing; (c) lands [[DontFightTheFiscal]] as Muir's fiscal-primacy riff on Zweig; (d) documents the **pre-war ex-US outperformance** that repatriated into the US safe haven (MAG7 −6.86%, Nasdaq −1%, Canada +8.6%, World ex-US +9.8%, Nikkei +17%, Brazil +17.2% YTD into 27 Feb); and (e) provides bear-case quantitative fills — US jobs creation averaging **only 21.4k/month since start of 2025** with all growth in education/health services; U Mich Consumer Sentiment at a **quarter-century low**; 2026 tax refunds **~$30bn** vs 2021's $200bn stimulus + $300bn UE.

## The bull case (Muir steelman)

### 1. Always buy the geopolitical angst ([[FadeGeopoliticalAngst]] / [[MarkoPapic]])
- BCA Research's canonical framework: geopolitical events rarely affect longer-term asset prices; if you are worried about an event there is usually little reason to position for it because it is already in the price.
- Muir admits he "strayed from this logic" on this war. In the bull-case framing: *"the buy the geopolitical angst trade worked in spades… it was just a little late in kicking in."*
- Linked: some long-term PMs are buying for a **50-year Mid-East prosperity** window ([[PeaceDividend]] via [[JamieDimon]]).

### 2. Earnings are rising ("the bulls' most powerful argument")
- *"Stocks follow earnings. It's that simple."* If you had one series to forecast stocks with, it's NTM consensus EPS.
- **S&P 500 NTM EPS keeps chugging higher** despite the war. Analysts continue to take numbers up.
- *"You can worry all you want about the economy. You can fret about oil prices. But if companies keep making money, investors won't sell stocks."*
- Muir explicitly labels this the strongest bull argument.

### 3. [[OneBigBeautifulBill]] fiscal is kicking in ([[DontFightTheFiscal]])
- No tax on tips / social security; attractive CAPEX capitalization tax rates.
- Designed for maximum current-year benefit (mid-term election timing).
- *"Fiscal trumps monetary policy."* The government's deficit = the private sector's credit (Kalecki-style identity).
- Muir's phrase coinage: *"If Marty Zweig was alive today, he'd have recoined his famous phrase as* **Don't Fight the Fiscal.***"*

### 4. The war hurts the US least → money returns home
- Pre-war YTD (through 27 Feb 2026):
  - MAG7: **−6.86%**
  - Nasdaq: **−1%**
  - Canada: **+8.6%**
  - World ex-US: **+9.8%**
  - Nikkei: **+17%**
  - Brazil: **+17.2%**
- During the war's first weeks: all markets down various amounts, no clear winner (except Brazil).
- **From 27 Feb to 17 April:** US stocks are the biggest winner.
- Bull interpretation: the 2025 "sell America, buy the world" trend reverses; repatriation back into the safe haven; America is energy-self-sufficient and thus less hurt by the energy shock.

### 5. Administration needs a hot economy for mid-terms
- Expectation of stimulus cheques and other supports ahead of autumn 2026 mid-terms.

### 6. AI worries are overblown
- Warren Pies chart: AI usage continues to surprise the bears.
- The past year of US underperformance may have been "just a pause in the longer-term trend of US technological dominance."
- SpaceX IPO still ahead as an additional catalyst.

## The bear case

### 1. Don't fade geopolitical angst (except maybe this time)
- Muir: *"I was bearish before the war, and even if the war ended tomorrow, I don't buy that the war has been a positive in terms of financial asset prices."*
- **Second-order reality:** the world now needs to increase military (especially navy) spending permanently → crowds out productive investment → long-term negative for financial assets.
- [[MarkoPapic]]'s own chart has exceptions (1940s gold) — sometimes the market takes a while to realize long-term pricing changes. Muir references his own [[buying-gold-again]] as precedent.
- A lot of "good news regarding an Iranian deal" is already priced in.

### 2. Earnings vs. free-cash-flow divergence
- NTM FCF is **not** rising at the same pace as EPS.
- **S&P 500 P/FCF is at the highest point of the last decade** (see [[PriceToFreeCashFlow]]).
- Risk that CAPEX doesn't produce the expected ROI is *"an underappreciated black swan paddling in the background."*
- DotCom analog: "it's difficult to know when free-cash-flow matters. However, when it comes to the forefront, the consequences will be devastating."
- And: markets historically anticipate the EPS slowdown before analysts lower forecasts.

### 3. [[EquityRiskPremium]] at multi-year lows
- ERP has not been lower since the initial days of the Trump administration (2025).
- Useless short-run, but shockingly accurate for multi-year returns.

### 4. Don't fight the fiscal (but the reality ≠ the hype)
- 2021 stimulus: **$200bn** in cheques + **$300bn** in extra unemployment insurance.
- 2026 tax refunds: **~$30bn.** "Still a lot of money, but nowhere near the 2021 stimulus."
- Odds of $2,000 cheques going out have "declined dramatically over the last month" — the Iran war is lasting longer than planned; Trump will need Congress for more military money first.

### 5. Economic precipice
- **U Mich Consumer Sentiment at a quarter-century low.**
- **US jobs creation since the start of 2025: average 21.4k/month** — "this is not a healthy labour market."
- All of the jobs are in **education and health services.** "Not representing a dynamic, growing economy."
- Beveridge Curve is poised for an unemployment rise.
- Pre-existing AI-driven uncertainty in the labour market is being compounded by the energy shock.

### 6. Flow of funds
- **Pre-war structural shift:** Trump policies forced the rest of the world to spend more; US historically ran 7%+ deficit/GDP while Canada/Europe/Japan ran sub-3% — this reversed, and money left the US for higher-return global markets in 2025.
- **War reversed the reversal** temporarily: safe-haven repatriation into the US.
- **American assets are the most over-owned in history** (per the chart Muir cites).
- Longer-term, *every* structural force points back the other way:
  - More military spending by non-US countries → more savings leaving US-held assets back to home economies.
  - More resource hoarding → capital tied up in home countries.
  - Financial repression globally (Korea's Dec 24 "bring home" tax rebate; Colombia's reduced foreign-asset allowance in pensions).
  - Globalization's reversal reduces the premium on American financial assets. "No country has adapted better to the current system than the US."

## Which side does Muir come down on?

- *"The chase higher has been nothing short of shocking."* Frantic buying "scares me rather than reassures."
- *"I am reminded that the sharpest rallies often occur within bear markets."* ([[AnatomyOfACrash]] relevance.)
- Acknowledges the tax-refund effect may have been larger than he realized; 2021-style manic speculation returned.
- Apparent removal of Mid-East risk (*"I'll leave it up to you to decide if that risk is as removed as Trump claims"*) makes the equity reaction "more sense than I initially thought."
- The 2025 flow reversal (US out, rest-of-world in) has been undone.
- **Admits the bear case is mostly long-term in nature and "none of those reasons seem to matter today."**
- **Concedes stocks may have lost their discounting mechanism** — the administration is "much more active in promoting stocks," participants see all glasses as half-full. *"I'd rather just acknowledge the reality of the market and trade accordingly."*

**But:** *"I am not giving up on my short US tech / long rest of the world ex-US trade. I had it on before the war, and view these past two months as emotional noise, muddling the issue."*

*"The bear case desperately needs a missing catalyst, but the overvaluation and crowded nature of long-term US positioning leaves me much more inclined to fade the herd than chase the latest hottest trend."*

## Contradictions
**Partial tension with [[PauloMacro]]'s [[NorthStar]], but not a contradiction:**
- Paulo ([[the-north-star-vs-liquidity]], 12 April): the bounce is explained by TGA liquidity + short-covering; hedges dumped; the North Star (Nash) is unchanged; TGA tailwind reverses after April 15 tax receipts.
- Muir (20 April): partially concedes the bull case has more substance than he credited — earnings are rising, flows have reversed, the market may have "lost its discounting mechanism." **But** he maintains the same trade structure.
- The two aren't in conflict: Paulo's frame is microstructural (liquidity + positioning); Muir's frame is fundamental (earnings, ERP, flows, labour). Both maintain short US-index exposure. Muir's candor about "acknowledge the reality of the market" is a healthier epistemic posture, not a different trade.

**Nuance to flag:**
- Muir explicitly labels rising EPS as *"the bulls' most powerful argument"* — a stronger concession than anything yet in the wiki. Contrast with Paulo ([[the-north-star-vs-liquidity]]): *"analysts still raising earnings estimates despite a 100% historical recession hit rate from oil deviation vs. trend."* The data is the same (rising estimates); Paulo treats them as late/wrong; Muir treats them as the strongest bull pillar. **This is the clearest inter-author calibration difference on the bull case so far.**
- The [[OneBigBeautifulBill]] fiscal argument introduces a bull pillar (fiscal stimulus) that was previously absent from the wiki. [[MichaelHowell]]'s [[GlobalLiquidityCycle]] framework is net-tightening globally; the US-specific fiscal injection is a potential counterweight at the domestic level.
