## Summary
[[RoryJohnston]]'s *Oil Ground Up* podcast with **[[AdiImsirovic]]** — Oxford lecturer, former head of oil trading, and author of *The Rivers of Money: Social and Economic History of Modern Oil Trading* — the wiki's **fifteenth author** and first dedicated physical-market microstructure specialist. Recorded around the second week of April 2026 (exact date inferred from internal references: $30 Dated-Brent-vs-futures spread consistent with [[macrovoices-528-rory-johnston]]'s 16 April observation; Brent futures "95ish" matches [[wctw-the-oil-market-breaking-point-is-here]] on 20 April). The conversation is the wiki's canonical reference for **how the [[BrentComplex]] actually works** — dated vs. forward vs. futures vs. CFDs vs. EFPs — and for **why the $30 Dated-Brent-over-futures spread is panic, not market dysfunction**. Imsirovic's memorable coinage: the "[[BrickAndMortarTrade]]" — refineries paying any price for physical barrels because running out means weeks of shutdown.

## Key Claims

### The $30 Dated-Brent-futures spread is panic, not dysfunction
- Dated Brent and Brent futures **are not the same thing** and are not comparable apples-to-apples:
  - Dated Brent = **physical**, with nominated loading dates 10–30 days forward.
  - Brent futures = **financially settled**, normally trading two months forward.
  - Comparing them is *"apples and oranges, the same commodity in a very, very different time period."*
- In backwardation, dated is always higher than futures. The steeper the backwardation, the bigger the spread.
- **The $30 spread is unprecedented** — *"I'd never seen [it]."* But it's driven by panic and scarcity on the front end, not a broken market.
- Imsirovic: *"The markets are, to be honest, working as well as they can under these crazy conditions."*

### The refinery death-spiral mechanic (same as [[CrackSpreads]] from the other side)
- Refineries shut down below 50–60% operating capacity; restart takes weeks.
- When told "get me oil," refinery traders go out and pay whatever it costs. This is the [[BrickAndMortarTrade]].
- In Northwest Europe specifically (per Rory): crude traders paying so much over Dated Brent that hedge-adjusted refinery margins are actually *negative*. One European refinery in the news had derivatives team hedging a "$30+ margin" on futures while the physical team was buying crude at $50 over futures → **net negative margin**.
- This becomes the refining death-spiral that Paulo's [[CrackSpreads]] rollover argument describes from the financial-derivative side. Two authors, two directions, one mechanism.

### The full price stack at the peak
Example reconstruction from Imsirovic (late-March/early-April):
- **Brent futures** (front-month): baseline reference.
- **+$30 Dated Brent** over futures (the "generic" physical premium).
- **+$15 Focados over dated** (Nigerian distillate-rich crude premium — distillate-short market).
- **+ freight + insurance** (~$5).
- **= ~$50 over futures** for a physically delivered Focados barrel.
- This is with Brent futures at ~$95 → effective delivered cost ~$145/bbl. Matches HSBC CEO's $286/bbl Sri Lanka quote in [[macrovoices-528-rory-johnston]] (a worse-than-average delivery).

### The June/December futures spread was $20
- Even within the futures curve itself, June-vs-December trades at $20 backwardation. *"I can't remember ever seeing that."*
- Front-month was clearly screaming scarcity *independent of the dated complex*.

### EFP normalization ratio
- **Exchange of Futures for Physicals (EFP):** normally 4–5 cents/bbl to convert a Brent futures contract to a physical cargo. During the crisis, peaked above **20 cents** for June; came back to ~14-15 cents.
- An EFP price ~4× normal is a simple way to measure the paper-vs-physical premium divorced from curve shape.

### Why the curve shape matters — and what it does *not* say
- Imsirovic pushes back on naive readings of the futures curve:
  - **Kevin Hassett** (US policymaker) quoted as saying *"the futures curve says oil will be ~$70 by next summer"*. Imsirovic: the futures curve is *not* a flat-price forecast. It encodes the necessary seasonal storage incentives (for gasoline/jet in summer, distillate in winter, etc.) that the market uses to rationally carry inventory across time.
  - **[[IEA]]'s Fatih Birol** saying *"futures markets will converge with physical markets"* is, per Imsirovic, "confusing the hell out of everyone." Futures always converge with physical on delivery month — because futures become physical. But **Dated Brent stays above the futures strip** because the *next* month's Dated takes over; the whole backwardation curve just shifts forward month-by-month as long as the tightness persists.

### What happens when (if) war resolves
- **The flat price falls dramatically** — potentially to $50–$60 oil "by the end of summer" on Imsirovic's view (Rory thinks $50 is aggressive; agrees on the directional collapse).
- **Backwardation stays** because of physical bottlenecks — Fujairah damage, shipping complex disruption, storage logistics, insurance issues. The *whole curve shifts upward or downward together*, not with the front collapsing to the back.
- Imsirovic: *"People will overpay, market will overshoot. And hopefully this war ends. If and when it ends, we'll be not just relieved, but the market will probably undershoot as well."*
- This is the cleanest articulation in the wiki of why Brent futures at $95 is *inconsistent* with the dated panic — the market is pricing a resolution that restores supply eventually, but the *physical market knows* there are post-peace bottlenecks that paper hasn't absorbed.

### The asymmetric price-action from Trump jawboning
- Rory observation that Imsirovic endorses: speculative longs repeatedly got "blown out" during rallies to $110-$115 when Trump tweets sent prices down $15-$20 in a day.
- The asymmetry is structural: *we don't vertical-spike up $20 in a day, but we can vertical-drop $20 in a day on a tweet.* This creates an unwillingness to hold paper long risk even while physical longs must be held because refineries can't run out.
- **Implication:** the futures complex is a degraded risk-management tool under these conditions — paper hedgers can't run physical length without policy risk; physical players are exposed either way.

### Saudi and UAE operational praise
- Saudi "ramped up the East-West pipeline to 7 million barrels [per day]" — *"that's incredible."*
- UAE / ADNOC "pumping 1.7 million barrels per day" via [[UAE]]'s Fujairah in spite of Fujairah fire damage. *"That's just incredible. Saved a lot of people's bacon."*
- Rory endorses — operational response was remarkable.

### Theory-of-storage literacy
- Imsirovic: **Contango is the natural state of commodity markets**; backwardation is "wrong way around" (hence the etymology).
- Flags [[IlyaBouchouev]] (friend, options specialist) as holding the opposite view — *"a Keynesian view"* where backwardation is natural because producers hedge down the curve more than end-users hedge up.
- Both views defensible; debate reflects the classical theory-of-storage vs. Keynesian normal-backwardation divide in commodity-market academics.
- Rory's self-developed split-regression framework: (a) inventory level → back-of-the-curve flat price via "revealed scarcity"; (b) supply/demand balance → front-minus-back spread structure via theory-of-storage mechanics. Imsirovic endorses as "got the whole thing."

### Volatility and monetary-policy as oil-price drivers
Memorable Imsirovic frame:
- *"Oil does not give you a dividend… gold is the same. You don't get a dividend. So the only thing you have is capital gains. Everything else is cost. So the higher the cost of storage, the higher cost of time value of money, the lower the oil price by definition."*
- **Interest rates are the cost of storing oil.** Higher rates = higher cost of carry = lower clearing price, all else equal.
- **Volatility in monetary and economic policy is itself bearish for oil price** — because it destabilizes the cost-of-carry side of the storage equation and pushes up risk premia.
- This is the first wiki-source articulation of the *monetary-policy-vol → oil-price* channel, complementary to [[OilPriceRecessionDebate]]'s supply-shock-vs-CB-response debate.

## Contradictions
- **Saudi East-West pipeline capacity: Imsirovic 7mmbpd vs. [[HFIResearch]] 4mmbpd** ([[wctw-the-oil-market-breaking-point]], 26 March). Most-likely reconciliation: Imsirovic quotes the pipeline's rated/ramped capacity; HFI reports observed tanker loadings off Yanbu. The two numbers measure different things. Alternatively, capacity may have ramped between late March and mid April. Flagged as the wiki's first real numerical tension between two oil-specialist authors — likely definitional rather than factual.
- **UAE production via Fujairah: Imsirovic 1.7mmbpd vs. [[HFIResearch]] 1.8mmbpd vs. [[PauloMacro]] 1.6mmbpd.** All three within a ~10% band; no analytical disagreement; timing artifact.
- **Post-war price path: Imsirovic $50-$60 by end of summer** under a war-resolution scenario. Rory pushes back ("surprised to see $50 given 450mmbbl already lost"). This is a *calibration disagreement*, not a mechanism disagreement — both agree the flat price would collapse while backwardation persists.
- **Contango-as-natural-state vs. backwardation-as-natural-state** (Imsirovic vs. [[IlyaBouchouev]]): the wiki now explicitly holds both positions as defensible within a single source page, reflecting the genuine academic divide.
