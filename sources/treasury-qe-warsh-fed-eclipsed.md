2. **"Treasury QE"** ([[TreasuryQE]]) — explicitly defined: (a) skewing debt issuance toward bills (currently 22.2% of total debt); (b) volatility-targeting buybacks that suppress the [[MOVEIndex]]. *"Bill issuance is stealth monetization. Banks buy bills, M2 grows, and the economy gets a stimulus — without the Fed lifting a hand."* Both mechanisms by-pass dealer-balance-sheet constraints.
3. **The Collateral Multiplier framework** ([[CollateralMultiplier]]) — Global Liquidity ($190T) sits on a $150T collateral pool with multiplier ~1.1×. Determined by *intensive* margin (haircuts) and *extensive* margin (re-hypothecation), both of which are strongly anti-correlated with bond volatility (MOVE/multiplier correlation = **−0.67**). **Each 10-point fall in MOVE → ~$3 trillion (1.5%) of automatic Global Liquidity creation, regardless of where the Fed sets rates.** This is the *quantitative* mechanism that makes Treasury vol-targeting more powerful than rate cuts.

The piece reframes the wiki's prior bank-reserves-centric narrative: **"The critical issue is not the volume of banks' reserves. The "Stealth" Accord exists because of this paradox — *"scarce intermediation capacity co-exists with the Fed's provision of ample reserves."*

The system's linchpin has moved from reserves to dealer balance sheet capacity and the MOVE index."* Setting the Fed funds rate is *"a pantomime"* — *"a sideshow."*

The fragility: two structural risks. **Risk 1 — Moral hazard:** markets expect Treasury buybacks → encourages excessive leverage in [[BasisTrade]]. **Risk 2 — Collateral fragility:** if MOVE spikes despite buybacks (e.g., geopolitical shock or dealer solvency event), the collateral multiplier collapses *mechanically* — haircuts rise, re-hypothecation reverses, Global Liquidity contracts. A "March 2020 COVID spiral" is the precedent.

## Key Claims

### The Stealth Accord — the hidden 2026 regime change

> *"For years, investors assumed the Federal Reserve was the driver of US growth, either through 'Fed QE' or through rate cuts. But quietly, starting in 2025, a regime change took hold. The Fed is no longer in charge of growth: the US Treasury is."*

The 1951 Fed-Treasury Accord *separated* monetary policy from debt management. The 2025/26 "Stealth" version *merges them back together*:

| | Driver | Tool | Visible? |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1951 Accord | Fed for monetary policy / Treasury for debt management | Fed funds + reserves | Yes (formal) |
| **2025/26 "Stealth" Accord** | **US Treasury for growth / Fed for financial stability** | **Bill issuance + buybacks** | **No (informal/de-facto)** |

The Stealth Accord is the *political-economy explanation* for why a Trump-era Fed Chair pivot does not necessarily change the macro regime: by the time Warsh arrives, the levers are already at Treasury.

### The dealer-balance-sheet primary constraint — wiki reframe

The wiki has previously emphasized **bank reserves** (via Paulo's [[LiquidityMechanics]] and the eSLR-driven swing in [[ReserveManagementPurchases]]) as the constraint. Howell now reframes:

> *"The critical issue is not the volume of banks' reserves. It is the capacity of dealers' balance sheets to warehouse Treasuries and intermediate repo flows."*

Dealer-banks face Basel III / eSLR caps. They cannot simultaneously:
- Absorb heavy Treasury issuance, AND
- Fund private markets

This produces the wiki's clearest articulation of the modern coordination paradox: **"scarce intermediation capacity co-exists with the Fed's provision of ample reserves."** Reserves abundance does not solve the problem if dealers cannot warehouse Treasuries.

> *"Think of the SOFR as more than just an interest rate, because it also measures the price of balance sheet capacity."*

### Treasury QE — operational definition

Two pillars, both designed to bypass dealer constraints by suppressing the MOVE index:

#### Pillar 1: Bill skew
- Bills now represent **22.2% of total debt outstanding.**
- Why bills work: only the *public* sector creates *net credit* (assets for the private sector with no matching liability). Short-duration government liabilities = liquidity boost.
- *"Bill issuance is stealth monetization. Banks buy bills, M2 grows, and the economy gets a stimulus — without the Fed lifting a hand."*

#### Pillar 2: Volatility-targeting buybacks
- Treasury buys less-liquid off-the-run bonds and replaces them with new on-the-run securities → lifts market liquidity → suppresses MOVE.
- **The empirical rule: each 10-point rise in MOVE → ~$30bn increase in buyback volumes.**
- This is the *Treasury-side coordination dial* that mirrors the Fed's [[ReserveManagementPurchases]].

### The Collateral Multiplier — the quantitative core

The wiki's first explicit framework for *how Global Liquidity is created from the collateral pool*:

```
Global Liquidity ($190T) = Collateral Pool ($150T) × Multiplier (~1.1×)
```

The multiplier is determined by:
- **Intensive margin** = the haircut dealer-banks apply to posted collateral.
- **Extensive margin** = re-hypothecation rate (how many times the same collateral is re-lent).

Both margins are **strongly anti-correlated with bond volatility** (MOVE):
- **Empirical correlation: −0.67** between MOVE and changes in collateral multiplier.
- **Each 10-point fall in MOVE → ~$3 trillion (1.5%) of additional Global Liquidity.**

> *"A lower MOVE index virtually assures automatic liquidity creation, regardless of where the Fed sets rates. **Is this why the Treasury now obsesses over volatility?**"*

### Comparing Fed QE vs Treasury QE

The piece's most important chart: Fed QE (red and orange — balance sheet expansion + compositional changes / "Not QE, QE") **declining** through 2026; Treasury QE (buybacks + bill issuance) **rising strongly**.

> *"The US Treasury has replaced the Fed as the marginal driver of US and Global Liquidity."*

> *"Neither party, Fed or US Treasury, admits to monetizing debt. But these policies facilitate and encourage precisely that."*

### Risk 1 — Moral Hazard

> *"Markets have come to expect Treasury buybacks whenever volatility rises. This can encourage excessive leverage in hedge fund basis trades. Why hedge and avoid volatility when the Treasury caps it for you?"*

This generalizes [[LukeGromen]]'s [[BasisTrade]] / Cayman Islands observation: **hedge funds are now the marginal buyers of Treasuries** because foreign demand (currency-hedged) has faded. They use cash Treasuries as collateral to borrow in repo markets, funding leveraged derivative positions — basis trades, yield-curve arbitrage, even crypto and stock futures.

The Stealth Accord *creates* this exposure by underwriting low MOVE. Vol suppression → leverage encouragement → larger eventual unwind.

### Risk 2 — Collateral Fragility

> *"Global dollar funding requires the MOVE index to remain around 80. Why? Because a stable collateral multiplier — and its US$3 trillion per 10-point MOVE sensitivity — requires a low and equally stable volatility regime."*

The mechanical chain when MOVE spikes despite buybacks:
1. Collateral multiplier collapses.
2. Haircuts rise.
3. Re-hypothecation reverses.
4. Global Liquidity contracts.
5. Hedge funds face rapid deleveraging → forced selling of Treasuries into illiquid market.
6. SOFR spikes → further haircut increases → spiral.

**Howell's precedent: March 2020 COVID emergency.**

> *"The Accord suppresses MOVE to manage volatility and prevent spikes — but that very suppression creates the complacency that could make the next spiral worse."*

### What this means for Chair Warsh

> *"Chair Warsh opposed QE in the 2010s, warning of excess and inflation. With hindsight, he was likely right. But now drastically shrinking the Fed's balance sheet, as mooted, and nostalgically returning to a 'scarce reserves' regime is no longer possible. This is because the system's linchpin has moved from reserves to dealer balance sheet capacity and the MOVE index."*

Warsh's real test:
- The US Treasury is in charge of growth.
- The US Fed manages the financial market side-effects.
- The big risk is a volatility shock that could reverse everything overnight.

> *"Watch the MOVE index. Watch SOFR spreads. Watch both for signs of stress in order to protect your wealth. Setting the Fed funds rate is already a pantomime, but it is also fast becoming a sideshow."*
