## Summary
[[MichaelHowell]]'s 4 April 2026 follow-up (Part 2 of 2): the transmission mechanism and warning indicators for the approaching crisis. The modern financial system is a **circular relationship between debt and liquidity with two legs**: the *Refinancing Leg* (right-hand side: when disrupted → credit spreads blow out, term premia collapse) and the *Collateral Leg* (left-hand side: when disrupted → repo spreads spike, bond vol/MOVE jumps → haircuts increase → collateral multiplier shrinks → less liquidity). Howell then identifies **four structural changes since the GFC** ("New Fault Lines") that make the next crisis a **"Not-GFC, GFC"** — not a bank-funding sudden stop (banks are shackled by regulation), but a **Treasury or credit market seizure** where liquidity vanishes among NBFIs, forcing Fed intervention at a scale that dwarfs 2008/09. Closing: *"Bull markets still climb a wall of worry, but financial crises increasingly smash into a wall of debt."*

## Key Claims

### Two-leg circular model
1. **Refinancing Leg** (right-hand side): debts mature → need balance-sheet capacity (liquidity) to refinance → when disrupted: credit spreads blow out, term premia collapse as flight to quality. Falls in term premia = early warning.
2. **Collateral Leg** (left-hand side): liquidity creation needs quality debt as collateral → transacted via repo (~$10–12T in US alone) → when disrupted: SOFR-IORB spread spikes, MOVE index jumps. Higher bond vol → bigger haircuts on collateral → reduced collateral multiplier → shrinking liquidity. This is the *pro-cyclical feedback loop*.

### Warning indicators
- **Term premia:** Falls = investors becoming more risk-averse, seeking safety of long-duration govvies.
- **SOFR less IORB spread:** "Latest data sit a tad above the normal zone, but earlier liquidity shortages in late 2025 have recently led to tensions." (This is exactly what Paulo documents in [[liquidity-drains-redux]] as repo rates ticking toward the top of the Fed corridor.)
- **MOVE index:** Governs haircuts on collateral; the key feedback mechanism for the collateral leg.

### Four "New Fault Lines" since the GFC

#### 1. US Treasuries — the fragile anchor
- Marketable US Treasury debt has grown **5.3x** since end-2008; bank credit has barely doubled. Treasury debt is now **2.3x** bank credit (was 0.8x pre-crisis).
- The Treasury market has shown "alarming fragility" (2019 repo crisis, March 2020 COVID meltdown).
- Reduced dealer balance sheets mean liquidity depends on hedge funds and algo traders who pull back in stress.

#### 2. Unregulated shadow finance (NBFIs)
- Pension funds, hedge funds, insurers, SWFs, and CICPs now dominate markets. Balance sheets grew **3x faster** than traditional banks since the GFC.
- More pro-cyclical, more sentiment-sensitive, more prone to herding (RORO). Less regulated, no direct CB backstop, yet more interconnected (BIS: 50% rise in interconnectedness since GFC).
- **Thin or zero haircuts** on repo collateral have boosted leverage → risk of vicious margin-call / fire-sale spiral.

#### 3. Cross-border flows shifted
- Pre-GFC: EM official purchases (China, Emerging Asia) financed US deficits.
- Post-GFC: advanced-economy **private** investors (European pension funds, Asian insurers) are the largest net inflows into US. Two-way spillovers (e.g., yen carry trade unwinds affecting US yields).

#### 4. More active central banks — blurring fiscal/monetary lines

### The "Not-GFC, GFC"
- A sudden stop in bank funding is unlikely (banks are tightly regulated post-GFC).
- Instead: a **Treasury or credit market seizure** where liquidity vanishes, creating distress among NBFIs and forcing rapid, large-scale Fed intervention.
- The Fed must now backstop **both** bond markets and banks.
- **$111T of FX derivatives** — 90% USD-denominated, mostly off-balance-sheet and unregulated.
- **Potential triggers:** external shock (Eurozone debt crisis, yen spike), derivative blow-up (FX swap mismatches), fiscal panic (US debt sustainability buyers' strike).

### [[KevinWarsh]] reference
*"The proposal by incoming Fed Chair Warsh to reduce the size of the Fed's balance sheet appears increasingly risky."*

### Policy prescription (Bagehot)
- The system needs **permanent market-makers** (standing repo facility as permanent Fed backstop for Treasury liquidity).
- NBFI regulation.
- Acknowledge USD dominance and monitor derivative structures / cross-border flows.
- *"In the short term, the remedy is to explicitly manage liquidity conditions to maintain a stable [[DebtLiquidityRatio]], rather than simply tweaking interest rates."*
- Interest rates are not the *price* of money but the *premium* on money. The true price is the exchange rate.
