## Summary
[[KevinMuir]] (the MacroTourist) argues against the sell-side consensus to buy the post-Iran/US/Israel-war dip in [[MAG7]]. While headline P/E valuations look attractive and the 20% correction mimics past buying opportunities, he warns that MAG7 has been the *worst*-performing bucket over the past six months despite record retail inflows, and — critically — that collapsing free cash flow from AI capex means MAG7 has **never been more expensive** on a Price-to-Free-Cash-Flow basis. He'd rather add to Japanese, Canadian, and Brazilian equities.

## Key Claims
- MAG7 is now trading at ~22.5x NTM EPS, a level that has marked buying opportunities in past cycles.
- Despite that, MAG7 has been the worst-performing major equity bucket over the past six months, even as public ownership hit record levels — a classic [[BruceKovner]] warning sign (heavily-owned theme failing to rally on good news).
- The "asset-light money machine" thesis for MAG7 is breaking down: [[AIInfrastructureInvestment]] is eating free cash flow, which is now collapsing.
- EPS forecasts assume sustained profitability on the AI build-out — an optimistic assumption that is hard to verify.
- The 2023 MAG7 bounce was partly fueled by mass layoffs dropping straight to the bottom line; that lever is gone because committed AI infrastructure is a fixed cost ("you can't fire servers").
- On a Price-to-Free-Cash-Flow basis, MAG7 **has never been more expensive** — the opposite of the bullish P/E narrative.
- Muir prefers adding to Japanese and Canadian equity longs and starting a Brazilian position over buying MAG7.

## Key Quotes
> "Being a late convert to a theme is dangerous — you risk getting stuck in 'broken themes' when the market has moved on." — [[ArrakisGlobal]], quoted by Muir

> "I don't see the attraction in buying the worst-performing market that is over-owned and trading poorly." — Muir on the MAG7 dip

> "You can't fire servers if business ticks lower." — Muir on why the 2023 layoff playbook won't rescue MAG7 this cycle

> "MAG7 is at best a trading sardine that will follow the general market. At worst, it's an over-owned, expensive sector that will be a source of cash…" — Muir's closing verdict
