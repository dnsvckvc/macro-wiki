## Summary
[[PauloMacro]]'s March 2024 primer on US system liquidity — the **origin document** for the [[LiquidityMechanics]] framework he later applies in [[the-north-star-vs-liquidity]] to explain the April 2026 risk rally. Self-deprecatingly disclaimed ("I have a bad take on liquidity, and damnit if I won't throw mine on the growing pile"), it walks through how the TGA, RRP, and bank reserves interact on the Fed's balance sheet, and makes the contrarian call that the April 2024 tax-payment liquidity drain would not be as bad as feared because Yellen would smooth it with Bills issuance timing. Includes a clean appendix defining the key identities.

## Key Claims
- **Liquidity formula:** Fed Balance Sheet (SOMA) − TGA − RRP ≈ system liquidity (assuming bank reserves stay flat).
- **TGA up = liquidity down** (Treasury issues debt, parks money at the Fed rather than spending). **TGA down = liquidity up** (Treasury spending without new issuance).
- **RRP decline = liquidity up** — the RRP was the Fed tacitly admitting it did trillions too much QE and needed to sequester it. Releasing the RRP balance releases past QE into the system.
- **Only two things really suck liquidity:** raising the TGA (Treasury places bonds but doesn't spend) or QT. The fiscal deficit itself is not a liquidity suck if Treasury spends immediately (money goes right back to bank accounts).
- **Yellen's playbook (2024):** smooth lumpy tax-payment flows by leaning on Bills issuance (which drains RRP rather than bank reserves) and holding off on coupon composition shifts. If tax revenues surprise low, the TGA actually *falls* — a liquidity injection.
- **RRP floor thesis skepticism:** "Nobody I've spoken to has been able to give me a logical reason as to why the RRP will find a floor at $300bn or $400bn." As long as Bills yield above the RRP rate, the outflow should persist.
- **The MMF practitioner's view:** a one-man-band PM running billions told Paulo the RRP is "a slight pain in the ass" — he'd happily just run a T-Bill ladder and skip it entirely if he could.
