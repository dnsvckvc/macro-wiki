## Summary

MacroVoices Episode 529 (23 April 2026) — feature interview with [[OleHansen]], Head of Commodity Strategy at [[SaxoBank]] (18 years), interviewed by [[EricTownsend]]. **Sixteenth author in the wiki.** Hansen brings the wiki's first dedicated *passive-investor* / *cross-commodity* perspective on the Iran energy shock: rather than physical microstructure ([[AdiImsirovic]]) or fiscal/dollar plumbing ([[LukeGromen]]) or supply-shortage math ([[HFIResearch]] / [[DrAnasAlHajji]]), Hansen frames the regime as a **multi-year tightening across energy, metals, and agriculture simultaneously** — the third leg of a commodity supercycle running on energy-transition demand against constrained supply. The interview's load-bearing technical contribution is **slide 4's Bloomberg Spot Index vs. Total Return Index decomposition**: 2016–2021 (mostly contango) spot +52% / total return only +14%; 2021–2026 (current backwardation regime) spot +57% / total return +83%. This is the wiki's clearest quantitative case for [[RollYield]] as a first-class portfolio driver. Plus: forward-curve chart on slide 7 ($12.50 backwardation June→Dec ≈ 15% annualized passive-long carry); the wiki's first detailed cross-asset chain — Middle East gas → fertilizer → wheat/corn (nitrogen-intensive) → food security; the **sulfuric-acid-from-Middle-East / copper-from-South-America** dependency Imsirovic and Rozencwajg never reached; classic cocoa supply-demand mean reversion; cotton-vs-synthetic substitution; and a fully-laid-out Patrick Ceresna **bull call spread on Dec 2026 WTI** that monetizes the backwardation directly. Hansen agrees with the rest of the wiki on direction (Brent floor moves from $70 to $80–$85; "two to three months from a peace deal" before normalization can begin) but adds the *passive-allocator* lens: even sideways spot prices now generate meaningful returns through the curve.

## Key Claims

### The energy shock breadth — beyond crude

- **Front-month futures look benign — but the disruption is "profound" and "broad."** Tightness sits in *refined products* (diesel, jet fuel, petrochemicals), not crude.
- The Persian Gulf has expanded beyond crude exports into **value-added refined and energy-intensive commodities**: aluminium, fertilizer (gas-feedstock), and—newly identified—**sulfuric acid** (50% of global supply from the Middle East), which copper miners in Chile, Peru, and Congo require to break down ore. Helium for chips also at risk.
- *"It's just the whole, the breadth of this crisis and how it impacts not only energy, but anything through to metals and the agriculture as well."*

### Forward-curve mispricing — Brent's new floor is $80, not $70

- Pre-war 2026 Brent traded $60–$75. Bear consensus (with [[IEA]] supply-glut narrative) was overdone; year started in a tight market.
- *"Once the dust settles… we should expect prices to settle in at least $10 higher level, maybe even $15 higher. So the floor has moved higher."*
- Dec 2026 Brent at $80 is mispriced — *"$80 is potentially where the new floor should be."*
- Normalization timeline: **two to three months from a peace deal** before any restart begins — ships out of position, refinery damages, oil tanks need to drain before producers can restart.

### Why no US producer response — backwardation as a brake

- Six weeks of crisis: **zero additional US barrels, zero additional rigs.**
- Hedging math kills the response: producers hedge 3–6 months out, where the curve is $10+ below front. *"The prices are still not that great."*
- Possibly also at the **shale saturation point** — *"are we getting close to a saturation point in terms of how high U. S. production can actually go at this stage?"*

### Backwardation as a portfolio driver — the slide-4 decomposition

This is the wiki's canonical [[RollYield]] case study.

| Period | Curve regime | Bloomberg Spot Index | Bloomberg Total Return Index | Difference |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 2016–2021 | mostly contango | +52% | +14% | **−38pp drag** |
| 2021–2026 | mostly backwardation | +57% | +83% | **+26pp tailwind** |

- ETFs/swaps roll futures continuously. Backwardation = sell expiring contract high, buy next contract low → **positive roll yield**. Contango is the inverse — *"that's basically why natural gas is such a dog to trade from a long-term perspective."*
- *"That massive difference is basically because we had a period, a number of years, where most markets were trading contango… Once again, if you look at the spot index, the yellow line, it has basically more or less done the same performance, up 57% against the 52% in the previous five years. But if you look at the total return, you're actually up 83%."*

### The current Brent forward curve (slide 7)

- ~$12.50 backwardation June → December.
- *"If you bought the December contract and waited for it to come to expiry in six months, you'd be making 15% on that investment in six months. So what does that annualize to? A whole lot."*
- Roll mechanics: rolling June ($95) into July ($90) gives an instant $5 if spot stays unchanged.
- Speculative overlay (right-hand chart): hedge funds went from **net short** at start of year (*"I can't recall I've ever seen hedge funds being so weak"*) to ~500mn barrels long; positioning concentrates at front-end where liquidity is deepest, amplifying both backwardation *and* the $5–$10 corrections from forced exits. *"Hedge funds, they're never, ever married to their positions."*

### Iran-conflict duration — pricing scaling problem

- Eric: "the tankers that left the Persian Gulf at the end of February are just now arriving at their destination. So the big disruption of supply is only about to start."
- Hansen agrees: *"Once that [hedge-fund liquidation] is done, the market will very, very quickly turn around and ask the hard questions… we will have to live with higher for longer."*
- *"That's the beauty of backwardation, how it works over time."*
- Friday spike low to $77 — *"below 80, there is some value to be found in Brent."*

### Fertilizer / agriculture — the next leg

- Nitrogen-intensive crops (wheat, corn) most vulnerable; soybeans least.
- *"It really only takes one bad season for that whole equation to change around."*
- US: dry start, mild winter end → wheat belt struggling already, US natural gas dirt cheap.
- Geographic propagation: **Northern hemisphere planting is already done** — fertilizer disruption now hits **India, Africa first**, then **South America** (now the biggest exporter to China) for the next planting cycle.
- 12-month performance check: **agriculture sector total return only +1.3% (1y) / +5% (2y)**. Multi-year lows, late on the supercycle. *"Metals were the driver last year. Energy was the driver at the start of the year. Potentially agriculture become another driver as we move into the second half and into 2027."*
- Curve regimes:
  - **Soybean oil** — already in backwardation (biofuel/diesel link).
  - **Wheat / corn** — still ~10% one-year contango.
  - **Coffee** — decent backwardation.
  - For agriculture, *"moving towards anything less than 5%, which is basically a reflection of the one-year funding cost… means we are moving towards a tightening market."*
- Gold doubling = *"fine."* Wheat doubling = *"complete different in terms of global food security."*

### Eric's pairs-trade idea — long Dec 26 wheat / short Nov 26 soybeans

- Wheat current front month: ~$6 / bushel. December contract (new crop): ~$6.40. Modest contango.
- Hansen: directionally reasonable on a pure nutrient-balance basis. But: *"if you want to trade what's coming out of the ground in the coming months, you will be looking at December corn, December wheat, and November soybeans."*

### Inflation regime — secular, self-reinforcing

- *"Just-in-time to just-in-case"* (citing [[JeffCurrie]] via Eric): the regime change in supply chains drives demand for higher inventory levels everywhere. Hansen: *"That basically means there will be a demand… inventory levels need to be kept around the world at higher levels than it was in the past."*
- Bloomberg Commodity Index has risen **~160% from the 2020 pandemic low.** *"The argument [for hard assets] probably has only been strengthened by developments in the last month."*
- Central banks stuck between two chairs — inflation focus or growth support — plus deteriorating fiscal debt situation.

### Commodity super-cycle (slide 3) — five years in, the third wave

- Three historical super-cycles: post-WWII reconstruction; 1970s inflation; 2000s China industrialization.
- *"What can we call the third wave? I think we can probably call it the energy transition."*
- *"We are in a power-hungry world… the old world is striking back against the new world"* (also citing Currie).
- Halfway in (5 of 10 years), but possibly longer. The cure-for-high-prices-is-high-prices logic applies in some markets but **the supply side may not be able to respond** in energy and metals: *"prices could actually still go up, even though economic growth is not great… the demand for many key commodities is at a level where the physical world is struggling to keep up."*
- Cocoa is the counter-example: 2,500 → 12,000 → "utterly collapse back to where we came from" — supply *and* demand responded.

### Gold — March 2nd panic flush

- Gold flipped from geopolitical hedge to liquidation candidate when the war started. Hansen: *"The market panicked… positions… so widely held investment, meaning that they were also exposed when that situation unfolded."*
- Pattern matches Liberation Day, dot-com bubble, GFC: initial gold sell-off → strong recovery to new highs in subsequent quarters.
- $1,500 correction; held the 200-day moving average at the 50% retracement.
- Critical level: **$4,685** (38.2% Fib retracement). Hansen: *"if we start to break back below the 4,500–4,600 area, then I would also get a bit nervous."*
- Dollar overlay: short-covering complete; biggest dollar long in two years from biggest short. Beginning to taper. Gold should resume.

### Copper — the better trade

- Less volatile than gold (high copper would jeopardize CapEx programs anyway).
- March low = exact 200-day MA. Recovery has been strong.
- **Sulfuric acid dependency** — ~50% of global seaborne sulfuric acid out of the Middle East; required for ore processing in Chile/Peru/Congo. Genuine supply-side constraint emerging.
- China: massive surge in exchange-monitored stocks early year (London + NY + Shanghai), but Shanghai inventories now drawing hard; **paying premium to import** = pent-up demand emerging post-correction.
- *"Suppliers, miners, will struggle and demand will remain robust to rising."*

### Cocoa — classic super-cycle round trip

- Production problems in Ivory Coast / Ghana (rain then drought) on a depressed base → 2,500 → 12,000 spike.
- Demand response: chocolate manufacturers reduced cocoa content, shrinkflation; **European grindings to lowest since 2013**.
- Supply response: high prices reached farmers → production ramped.
- Now over-supplied; sets up the next spike.
- Speculative tangent: an Israeli company developing **lab-grown cocoa**.

### Cotton — the oil substitution chain

- Direct fuel-to-fiber link: **synthetic fiber is a petrochemical product.** When synthetic-fiber cost rises, demand substitutes back into real cotton.
- Reinforced by drought conditions in US winter wheat regions (also cotton areas).
- Sugar/ethanol parallel: Brazilian sugar cane allocates between **ethanol** (biofuel) and sugar; high oil drags sugar prices up via substitution.

### Patrick's Trade of the Week — bull call spread on Dec 2026 WTI

- Spot WTI ~$94; Dec 2026 WTI ~$77.40 (211 days to expiry).
- November 2026 options (208 days):
  - Buy $70 call @ $11.70 ($7.40 intrinsic, $4.30 time value)
  - Sell $90 call @ $4.40
  - **Net debit $7.30; spread width $20**
- Break-even ~$77.30 ≈ underlying.
- **Max loss $7.30; max profit $12.70.**
- Premium "largely intrinsic value" → low vega, minimal time decay → effectively defined-risk high-delta exposure to the deferred curve. The trade structure monetizes Hansen's "Brent/WTI floor has moved higher" thesis without front-month volatility.

### Patrick's S&P / hedge / overall market read

- 23-day April bull run, +13%, near 52-week highs; driven by systematic flows (CTA flip / vol normalization / dealer-gamma collapse), now mostly behind us.
- Earnings of five MAG7 next week determine whether the market pushes to 7,400–7,500 or reverses.
- Eric's hedge: **6,800–6,000 bear put spread on S&P futures** (replaced his expired hedges).

### Eric's Iran-shutdown timeline math

- JPM: Iran can withstand ~2 more weeks of US blockade before forced shut-ins.
- Once shut in, wells take **months** to bring back online; 3–4 month restart lag for Iraq/Kuwait already documented in the wiki.
- **Two more weeks of blockade → 2–3 more months of disrupted supply.** Multiplier effect.
- *"We could have six to 12 months of dramatically elevated energy prices globally, completely screwing up the entire global economy."*

## Contradictions

- **No load-bearing contradictions with prior wiki sources.** Hansen's $80 Brent floor sits between [[RoryJohnston]]'s $130–$200 Dec-2026 path (Johnston more bearish for crude trajectory) and [[AdiImsirovic]]'s post-war $50–$60 flat-price call (Imsirovic more bearish for normalized state). Hansen's view applies to the *settled-floor regime after normalization*, not the war-active period — closer to Imsirovic's $50–$60 in spirit but $20–$30 higher because Hansen treats the structural shift in supply chains as permanent.
- **Calibration nuance vs. [[LynAlden]]:** Hansen's secular-inflation read implies more inflation persistence than Alden's "spikes fade absent money-supply growth" framing — but the difference is timing-of-money-supply-response, not regime characterization.
- **The agriculture timing:** Hansen places agriculture as a *2H 2026 → 2027* driver. The wheat/fertilizer trades from Paulo ([[make-1973-great-again]]) were 1H 2026 calls. Hansen is implicitly saying the agriculture leg has not yet materialized in price — the *opportunity is still ahead*.
