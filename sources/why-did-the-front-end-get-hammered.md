## Summary
[[KevinMuir]]'s 27 April 2026 follow-on to [[making-sense-of-the-last-two-months]] — same author, same day as [[MichaelHowell]]'s [[crypto-gold-pboc-vs-fed]] (the Howell + Muir "double-header"). Muir's deeper-dive on **why front-end fixed-income was the worst-performing asset class through the war** — and, more importantly, **why the crude→front-end relationship is already breaking down in the last two weeks**, with major positioning implications for the next leg of the war. New material in this piece (relative to the 19 April retrospective): (a) the **canonical [[FedLookThrough]] case study** — a deep dive into 2007–2008 with FOMC minutes excerpts (Jan 2008 50bp cut, March 2008 75bp cut + Fisher/Plosser dissents, June 2008 hold-at-2% with oil at $145) showing the Fed *cut into a $55→$145 oil spike*; (b) the **[[RemovalOfEasingVsHikes]] framework** — Muir's most-load-bearing 2026 framework distinction: removing 75bps of priced-in easing is asymmetrically easier than actually raising 75bps ("Although there should be no difference between the theory of removing implied easing and the practice of raising rates, in practice, there is" — Muir's pseudo-Berra); (c) **regression evidence** that the Dec 2026 WTI ↔ Dec 2026 SOFR relationship has decoupled in the last two weeks (yellow-dot rates have sunk back to *pre-FOMC levels for a given level of crude*); (d) a **revised SOFR price path** vs the 19 April retrospective: pre-war "just under 3%" → **3.875%** at the war-peak (~87.5bps), with pre-war 6m crude $60 → $80 driving the move; (e) **STIR specialist hedge fund losses in March** as the positioning explanation — the rate move was *not* purely fundamental; (f) the **March 17–18 hawkish FOMC** as inflection ([[ChristopherWaller]] and [[MichelleBowman]] withdrew dissents; Waller follow-up speech confirmed Fed on hold); (g) Muir's view of *why this Fed was hawkish despite [[FedLookThrough]] precedent* — not political (DOJ overhang), but the [[ScottBessent]] / [[StephenMiran]] flak about being late on inflation in 2022/2023. **Trade implication:** the next crude spike *will not* produce the same front-end response. The crude-vs-SOFR regression line is breaking; if crude rips again to $90+, *the right trade is long Brent + long Dec SOFR*, not last-month's "sell front-end / buy USD." *"It's one thing not to ease, but it's completely another to raise rates."*

## Key Claims

### The unusual regime — higher energy + higher front-end rates + higher equities

> *"The market has assumed that higher energy prices will cause a hawkish Central Bank response (at least as compared to the previously expected Central Bank policy path), but that this won't result in slower growth going forward."*

Muir admits he would have gotten this combination wrong: *"I would have put a low probability on higher energy prices, higher short-end rates, and higher equity prices."* But this is exactly what printed.

### Revised quantitative front-end path

| Date | Dec 2026 SOFR implied rate | Dec 2026 6m WTI |
|---|---|---|
| Pre-war (Feb 2026) | "just a little under 3%" | $60 |
| Peak (3 weeks in / ~22 Mar) | **3.875%** | (war peak crude) |
| Last two weeks (yellow dots, mid-Apr+) | back to pre-FOMC levels | back near $80 |
| Current implication | only ~50bps moved | ~$20 above pre-war |

This is **a revision** of the data Muir gave on 19 April ([[making-sense-of-the-last-two-months]]: "Dec 2026 SOFR repriced 2.05% → 2.95%"). The 27 April numbers are the more focused/authoritative set since this piece is the dedicated front-end deep dive. Direction and magnitude are unchanged: ~85–90bps of forward-rate tightening sourced entirely from Dec 2026 crude.

### The hawkish March 17–18 FOMC

- **Waller and Bowman** (two of three doves who had previously dissented on the unchanged-policy decision) **withdrew their dissents** at the March 17–18 meeting — *"in essence, affirming the implied tightening."*
- A follow-up Waller speech confirmed the Fed was on hold.
- *"In hindsight, this was a hawkish FOMC meeting, and it likely contributed to the final sharp move higher in front-end rates and the subsequent end-of-March weak equity price action."*
- This dovetails with the [[making-sense-of-the-last-two-months]] timeline: 30 March was the SPX low / [[JPMCollarTrade]] roll day.

### Why this Fed was hawkish — not the look-through history

Muir spends a section on [[FedLookThrough]] — the canonical 2007–2008 example where oil ran from $55 to $145 and the Fed *cut* rates. Three FOMC minute extracts from this period:

1. **Jan 29-30, 2008** (50bp cut): *"Most members agreed that a 50 basis point easing at this meeting would likely not contribute to an increase in inflation pressures given the actual and expected weakness in economic growth."*
2. **March 18, 2008** (75bp cut): the Fisher/Plosser dissent argued for *less* aggressive easing; majority cut anyway because *"the Federal Reserve had already taken separate, significant actions to address liquidity issues."*
3. **June 24-25, 2008** (held at 2% with oil at $145): *"Headline inflation was generally expected to moderate in coming quarters, reflecting importantly an anticipated leveling-out of prices for energy and other commodities."*

> *"Time and time again, the Fed chose to find excuses to cut rates even as oil was soaring."*

Why this Fed broke from that pattern:
- **Not political** (DOJ Powell investigation): *"Powell has a strong sense of duty, and I am convinced he is always doing what he believes to be in the country's best interest."*
- **The 2022/2023 inflation flak**: [[ScottBessent]] and [[StephenMiran]] were "brutal in their complaints that the FOMC let inflation expectations rise too quickly." Muir's diagnosis: *"Central bankers, government officials, and regulators always fight the last war, so the perceived error from the last tightening campaign is likely having too great an influence."*

### The [[RemovalOfEasingVsHikes]] asymmetry — Muir's most load-bearing 2026 framework

> *"Although there should be no difference between the theory of removing implied easing and the practice of raising rates, in practice, there is."* (Muir's pseudo-Yogi-Berra)

> *"Allowing the market to remove 75 basis points of implied easing is a lot different from raising 75 basis points. Standing pat with monetary policy is infinitely easier than raising rates."*

- *Removing easing:* the Fed declines to deliver previously-expected cuts. Market repricing absorbs it. No press-conference defense required. Citizens see "Fed neutral."
- *Actual hikes:* the Fed must publicly justify a tightening into an energy-supply shock. Bessent/Miran/Trump-era political pressure doesn't go away in this scenario; it goes the other way.

### The breaking crude→SOFR regression

Muir's core empirical claim — illustrated with two regressions:

**Regression 1: Dec 2026 WTI vs Dec 2026 SOFR** (Feb 28 → April 24, with last 2 weeks excluded as yellow dots)
- For Feb 28 → April 11 the relationship was tight: every $1 of crude lifted SOFR a measurable amount.
- The yellow dots (last 2 weeks) sit *below* the regression line — *"rates have sunk back to the pre-FOMC meeting levels for a given level of crude."*

**Regression 2: Dec 2026 WTI vs DXY** (orange dots = last 2 weeks)
- Same pattern — DXY weakening for a given crude level.
- *"For the period of February 28th to April 11th, 2026, crude oil was driving the bus when it came to setting front-end rates and the US dollar. However, the last two weeks have seen the relationship fade."*

The mechanical 6-month-crude → SOFR transmission rule of thumb: $60 → $80 crude moved SOFR back-up 75bps. Now we're back near $80 and the market has priced in only **50bps**. The relationship is degrading by ~33%.

### STIR specialist hedge fund unwind ([[STIRSpecialistHedgeFunds]] context)

Muir notes that part of the front-end blowout was *"too many hedge funds aggressively speculating on lower front-end rates."* Multiple STIR specialist hedge funds had large losses in March. **Positioning unwind made the response to the war all the worse.** This is the front-end version of the [[Degrossing]] story.

### The forecast: next crude spike will produce a different response

> *"In the coming weeks, if the war worsens and energy prices rise dramatically, the mistake will be assuming that the previous two-month relationship between crude and front-end rates stays strong. If anything, it will eventually reverse. The higher energy goes, the more the economy will slow, and the more the Fed will need to ease."*

Logic chain:
1. From here, the Fed cannot remove more easing — there's no easing left to remove (the 3 cuts already taken out, plus a hike chance priced).
2. Another crude spike would imply *actual hikes* — not "removal of easing."
3. The Fed will not actually hike into an energy supply shock (per [[FedLookThrough]] precedent).
4. So the front-end will not move proportionally on the next leg up in crude.
5. **In fact, beyond a certain point, the front-end should move *lower* on rising crude** because the implied recession case dominates the implied hawkish-Fed case.

### Trade ideas

Muir's two suggested expressions:
- **Sell Brent put spreads** (positive carry from the steep [[BrentComplex]] backwardation, elevated vol = good premium) **+ long Dec SOFR options** as the pair.
- **Or simpler:** long Brent + long the appropriate amount of Dec SOFR — bet the yellow dots are headed below the regression line.

Connects to the [[RollYield]] / [[BrentComplex]] work from [[OleHansen]] / [[AdiImsirovic]]: positive Brent roll-carry + elevated vol = an unusually attractive options seller's environment on the bullish side of crude.

### The ending wonder

> *"The big surprise of the past two months has been how front-end rates have risen while growth expectations have stayed elevated. I wonder if the surprise for the next two months will be how much that flips."*

## Contradictions

- **Numerical revision (not contradiction) of [[making-sense-of-the-last-two-months]]:** the 19 April retrospective said *"Dec 2026 SOFR repriced 2.05% → 2.95%"*; the 27 April piece says *"just under 3%" → "3.875%."* The 90bps directional conclusion is identical; the absolute levels differ. The 27 April numbers are more reliable since this is the dedicated front-end analysis with explicit charts. (The 2.05% pre-war figure is implausible for Dec 2026 SOFR with Fed funds at ~4.33% and only 2.5 cuts priced — likely a transcription error in the prior summary.)
- **Calibration update — not contradiction — to [[OilPriceRecessionDebate]]:** Muir's 30 March view was *"we don't get to run the experiment this time, because the market has already re-priced central-bank policy upward on its own. We get the energy spike and the rate rise."* The 27 April piece refines this: we got the both-legs version through April 11, but the second crude spike will likely be more Bernanke-style (look-through Fed) rather than Hamilton-style (rate response). This is consistent — Muir is updating which leg of the academic debate is operative for *the next move*, not changing his view of the prior move.
- **No load-bearing contradictions with [[PauloMacro]] or [[MichaelHowell]].** Muir's "front-end will move differently next time" is consistent with Paulo's [[NorthStar]] (Iran's Nash strategy unchanged → crude moves higher) and Howell's [[GlobalLiquidityCycle]] turning down (real economy still resilient, financial liquidity weakening) — all three converge on: the next risk-asset move down will come from a different transmission channel than the first one.
