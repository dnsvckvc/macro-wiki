## Summary
[[MichaelHowell]]'s 3 April 2026 analytical note (Part 1 of 2), published for institutional clients at CrossBorder Capital and made more widely available. Introduces his core quantitative framework: the **[[DebtLiquidityRatio]]** — the ratio of total debt to available Global Liquidity — as a systemic barometer for financial-crisis risk. The ratio averaged **2.0x** since 1980; peaked at **2.3x** during the 2010 Eurozone banking crisis; bottomed at **1.58x** in 2021 (the "Everything Bubble"). It is now projected to breach 2.0x in 2026 and retest 2.3x by 2030 as a **[[WallOfDebt]]** of ~$45T in advanced economies ($65T globally) comes due for refinancing just as the [[GlobalLiquidityCycle]] turns cyclically weak. Howell's thesis: *"Modern financial crises are fundamentally 'debt refinancing crises.'"* Another is approaching.

## Key Claims

### The core reframing
- Markets no longer primarily serve as **capital-raising mechanisms**. They have become systems for **servicing and refinancing** existing debt liabilities. ~70–80% of all primary transactions now refinance existing debts.
- In 1980, debt refinancing volume ≈ annual capex across advanced economies. By 2030, the debt maturity wall alone will be **~2.5x** annual new capex.
- Textbook models of interest rates slowing/accelerating the business cycle are obsolete. Central banks, leveraged hedge funds, sovereign wealth funds, and passive index managers dominate flows. Government interest bills have become a major source of private-sector income. The polarity of the financial system has flipped.

### The [[DebtLiquidityRatio]]
- Measures systemic balance: debt stock normalized by available Global Liquidity (not GDP).
- Long-term average: **2.0x (200%)**.
- Can be understood as aggregate portfolio duration vs. a target: when the ratio is stretched high, investors are forced to liquidate; when low, they extend duration → asset booms.
- **77% of global lending is now collateral-based** (World Bank), mostly transacted via repo markets. Paradox: *"New credits rely on the integrity of old debts."*
- Historical pattern: high ratio → crises (GFC, Eurozone); low ratio → bubbles (Everything Bubble, post-COVID).

### The four phases
1. **Post-GFC Liquidity Boom (2009–2025):** Massive CB injections + ZIRP/NIRP → debtors termed out borrowings → ratio collapsed to all-time low of 1.58x in 2021 → "Everything Bubble."
2. **Inflection (Present):** Liquidity still rising in absolute terms ($190T, +$17.2T over past 12 months) but growth rate slowing (peaked end-Q3 2025). Real economy competing with financial markets for liquidity. *"All money that is anywhere must be somewhere — when it funds real spending it cannot also drive asset prices."*
3. **The Coming Squeeze (Projected):** The [[WallOfDebt]] — COVID-era debts bunching for refinancing — will force the ratio above 2.0x in 2026 and toward 2.3x by 2030. "This occurs both because of a potential slowdown in liquidity growth and because of higher projected debt refinancing needs."

### Using Merton-style modeling
- Howell constructed a **probability-of-liquidity-crisis** indicator analogous to Merton's distance-to-default, extended to 2030. It last rose strongly through 2008, peaking in 2010 (Eurozone). Now rising again.

### Warning signs already visible
- Repo rate spikes (the 4Q25 episodes Paulo documents as quant quakes)
- MOVE index jumps
- Reversing Treasury term premia
- Concerns over private credit integrity
- All "spurred by latest Iran tensions"

## Contradictions
- None. **Strong quantitative confirmation of the qualitative thesis.** Where Paulo's Anatomy of a Crash and credit-cycle work are observational/anecdotal (AirBNB quotes gapping, private credit at 80–85% of NAV, cockroaches emerging), Howell provides the structural model showing *why* these strains are appearing: the debt/liquidity ratio is inflecting upward for the first time since the Everything Bubble bottom, driven by the simultaneous arrival of the Wall of Debt and the cyclical liquidity downswing. The two frameworks are orthogonal and mutually reinforcing — same conclusion, different analytical layer.
