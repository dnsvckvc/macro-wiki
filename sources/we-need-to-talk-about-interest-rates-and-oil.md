## Summary
[[KevinMuir]]'s 30 March 2026 macro essay answering the question: is it the **oil price spike** or the **central-bank response** that causes the recession? The ugly answer for markets: *we're getting both*. After the 28 Feb 2026 US/Israel military offensive against Iran, WTI ripped from $67.50 → $115 before an emergency global SPR release tamped price back to $93. More importantly, global front-end yields have *exploded higher* — the US has priced out all 2.5 cuts for 2026; the BoC has flipped from a small-chance-of-cut to 100% probability of 2 hikes / 60% of 3; the ECB from 50% chance of a cut to 100% of *three hikes*; the UK from 100% of 2 cuts to 100% of 3 hikes. Muir reviews the academic [[OilPriceRecessionDebate]] (Bernanke/Gertler/Watson 1997 vs. Hamilton/Herrera, with cameos from the Lucas Critique and [[LutzKilian]]), inverts in the [[CharlieMunger]] style to ask what would need to go right to avoid recession (he fades all three conditions), and reinforces his long fixed-income trade. Closes with the Angela Lansbury / *Murder, She Wrote* metaphor: rising energy prices are the killer on the scene *before* the crime — you don't know when, but you know someone is about to die.

## Key Claims

### War / oil timeline
- **28 February 2026:** US and Israel launched a military offensive against Iran. Most market participants had not expected military action this quickly after an earlier "Venezuela event"; pre-war focus was on AI disruption, not war.
- **WTI path:** $67.50 pre-war → peaked at $115 → tamped back to **$93** on an emergency global [[SPRRelease]].
- **[[StraitOfHormuz]] has now been under Iranian control for 30 days** as of 30 March 2026. No near-term opening in sight. Even if the warring parties came to a miraculous agreement, the damage to the energy shipping complex would take months to clear (per shipping expert [[SalMercogliano]] on Muir's "Watch Your Bids" podcast).

### Global bond market destruction
- US 2Y: the 2.5 Fed cuts priced for end-2026 have been *completely removed*.
- **Bank of Canada:** pre-war ~small chance of a cut → now **100% chance of 2 hikes, 60% chance of 3**.
- **ECB:** pre-war 50% chance of a cut → now **100% chance of 3 hikes**.
- **Bank of England:** pre-war 100% chance of 2 cuts → now **100% chance of 3 hikes**.
- 1Y inflation expectations have risen **~100bps** to **3.15%**.
- This is *not* happening because economies are running hot — the labour market is "precariously balanced" with hiring frozen pending clarity on AI disruption.
- Two of the three Fed doves ([[ChristopherWaller]] and [[MichelleBowman]]) withdrew their dissents at the most recent FOMC meeting — tacit endorsement of the hawkish shift.

### The age-old academic debate — [[OilPriceRecessionDebate]]
Does the oil *price spike* cause the recession, or the *central-bank response*?
- **Bernanke / Gertler / Watson (1997):** "Systematic Monetary Policy and the Effects of Oil Price Shocks" — argued it's the Fed's rate response, not the price itself.
- **[[JamesHamilton]] / Herrera (rebuttal):** pushed back on Bernanke's math and on central banks' ability to control rates cleanly.
- **Lucas Critique:** historical data can't predict the effect of a policy change because actors adapt.
- **[[LutzKilian]]:** differentiate between demand-led and supply-shock oil spikes.
- Muir's view: *we don't get to run the experiment this time*, because the market has already re-priced central-bank policy upward on its own. We get the energy spike *and* the rate rise. That's the worst combination.

### Historical precedents — oil spikes at major tops
[[RobertMcNally]] (Rapidan Energy Group) in a Bloomberg clip: *"An oil price spike is usually present at the scene of the crime. Oil demand is really inelastic. It is necessary for life… The only way to get demand down is to stop the work — to slow economic activity. So oil keeps on rising until it causes a demand slowdown, and then demand collapses. We saw that in 2008. We saw that in other times."*

- **2007–2008:** oil's contribution to tightening financial conditions ahead of the S&P 500's 56% decline.
- **1999–2001 (DotCom top):** less well remembered — oil rose **245%** from 1999 to 2001 and contributed to the 49% S&P decline.
- [[MichaelCembalest]]'s "Eye on the Market" chart (JPM Private Bank) catalogs the same pattern.

### Inverting the problem (Munger-style)
Muir asks: what would need to go right to avoid recession? He fades all three:
1. **Oil falls back to $50** — extremely low chance given Hormuz is still closed and the shipping complex damage will take months to clear.
2. **Bernanke is right, and the oil price rise itself is not the root cause** — and central banks don't tighten. Muir is "much more sympathetic to the idea that it's the energy price rise that causes the slowdown"; inverts again (*is falling energy good or bad for the economy?*) to confirm.
3. **Central banks hold the line on rates** — but the back-up in forward expected rates has already happened, and no central banks are pushing back. Even two of three Fed doves withdrew dissent.

### Fixed income is still the trade
- Muir loves owning long fixed income *because* the more rates rise in response to the oil spike, the more confident he is the economy gets pushed into recession — which eventually brings the cuts back.
- Distinction: *"Sticking out your hand to buy government fixed-income into an energy supply shock is much different from trying to fade a red-hot economy."*
- The bull case is "being eroded bit by bit." The labour market was already precariously balanced; the uncertainty from the war gives employers more reason to fire or freeze hiring; the consumer faces a new energy-price tax; unplanned war spending makes further stimulus politically difficult.
- Muir does not claim to know *when* stocks break — but the 2000 and 2007 charts both had plenty of up-months within the decline. The point is that oil spikes are "unequivocally bad for stock markets, and trying to rationalize it away is not being intellectually honest."
