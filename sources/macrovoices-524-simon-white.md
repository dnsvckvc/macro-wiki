## Summary
MacroVoices Episode 524 feature interview (19 March 2026) — paired with [[RoryJohnston]] in the same episode. [[SimonWhite]] (Bloomberg macro strategist, *Macroscope* column, *MarketsLive* blog) interviewed by Eric Townsend. The wiki's **eleventh author**. White provides the cleanest **1970s/stagflation framework** in the wiki: inflation as a **three-act play** (Act 1 first high = pandemic; Act 2 premature all-clear = last 2 years; Act 3 comeback-of-the-vengeance = starting now with Iran/Yom-Kippur analog). Key contributions: (1) **inflation is the most mispriced thing in markets** — CPI fixing swaps expect 3.5% peak → 2.8% within 12 months; breakevens barely moved; (2) the **food shock beat the energy shock** in 1970s CPI contribution and fertilizer leads food CPI by ~6 months — concrete mechanism confirming the wiki's Wheat thesis; (3) **[[KevinWarsh]] = Arthur Burns**, not Paul Volcker — dovish appointee into an inflationary regime; (4) **rewriting the risk-off playbook** — dollar won't rally as hard; commodities can rally through a commodity-induced recession; (5) **private credit = subprime 2007 echo** — bank lending to NBFIs has "mushroomed" (explicit calibration disagreement with [[LynAlden]]); (6) **gold as downside tail hedge AND inflation hedge.**

## Key Claims

### Three-Act Inflation Play (1970s analog)
- **Act 1 (first high):** Pandemic in 2020-21 (analog: Vietnam War + LBJ Great Society + Medicare fiscal easing).
- **Act 2 (premature all-clear):** Last 2 years — inflation supposedly transitory but stubbornly above target.
- **Act 3 (comeback of the vengeance):** Beginning now. Iran war is the Yom Kippur analog.
- The timing match is uncanny — "almost to the month" when Act 2 ends matches when attacks on Iran started vs. October 1973.
- 1970s resolution only came with **Paul Volcker's Saturday Night Special** rate hikes. Not happening with Warsh.

### Inflation is mispriced
- CPI fixing swaps: peak ~3.5% in coming months → back to 2.8% within 12 months (only 40bp above current).
- 2-5Y breakevens moved 20-50bp since war started; **10Y barely moved 5-10bp.** "Team transitory is back in force."
- *"Muscle memory is kicking back in that inflation will always go back to target. That's quite complacent."*

### Yom Kippur commonalities
- Surprise attack. Short war (~3 weeks). **Oil shock was *after* the war via embargo** — oil quadrupled in months despite ceasefire.
- Nifty Fifty equity market (narrow leadership) had already sold off 10-15% pre-war → **then another 45% in the year following.** Largest selloff since the Great Depression.
- Today: similar narrow leadership (MAG7 concentration), but with a bigger delta — CAPE 40 vs. 1973's 18; household allocation to financial assets much higher. Risk is asymmetric.

### Food shock > energy shock (CPI-weighted)
- In the 1970s, **food was a larger contribution to CPI than energy** in both OPEC 1 and OPEC 2 episodes.
- Food inflation was actually *rising before* the energy shock in 1973.
- Today: food contribution to US CPI is already *higher than energy*.
- **Fertilizer proxy** (urea, ammonia, sulfur, potash) leads food CPI by ~6 months. Strait disruption affects all of these inputs (Iran produces a lot of urea and ammonia; sulfur flows through the Strait). Mechanism confirms the wiki's Wheat thesis (see [[Wheat]], [[macrovoices-527-adam-rozencwajg]]).
- **Second-round effects into core inflation** likely → Fed's worst scenario: sticky stagflation, not transitory.

### [[KevinWarsh]] = Arthur Burns, not Paul Volcker
- Arthur Burns was at the Fed during Yom Kippur / OPEC 1. Dovish. Believed central banks couldn't do much about supply-shock inflation.
- Got staffers to invent **core inflation** so he could argue there was less to worry about than headline suggested.
- White thinks Warsh leans the same way — "nobody is sure he's going to be an inflation fighter; quite the opposite."
- This was also a decade of "go-stop monetary policy" — hike, loosen, hike, loosen — huge underlying volatility.

### Yield curve not pricing the shock
- Real yields rising (driven by higher rate *expectations*); breakevens muted.
- In OPEC 1: breakevens rose but real yields had an equal and opposite shock (textbook stagflation) → Arthur Burns kept front-end low → curve **steepened**.
- In OPEC 2: Volcker at the helm → front end up hard → curve **flattened**.
- White's call: **this time curve steepens like OPEC 1** (Warsh ≈ Burns).

### Risk-off playbook needs rewriting
- **Dollar:** In 2008 GFC, dollar rallied because of US-entity **repatriation** of European exposures. Today: bond flows smaller, equity flows much bigger and unhedged → reverse mechanism in a risk-off. Dollar rally might be much smaller or even absent. DXY only +1.5-2% since war started vs. the much larger 2008 move.
- **Commodities:** In a commodity-*induced* recession, commodities can sell off into the slump (softening the blow) and then **rally through** the recession. Not the standard "recession = commodities down."
- **Gold as dual hedge:** not just inflation hedge but **downside tail hedge** — the one asset with a proven track record in both debasement and deflation/credit-event scenarios. Structural demand from lack of alternatives; central banks won't sell (Poland mooting sale was for defense spending, not bearish on gold).

### US economy firing on all cylinders pre-war
- Business cycle: leading indicators turning up.
- Liquidity cycle: excess liquidity (real money growth minus economic growth) turned back up.
- Global cycle: OECD leading indicators turning up across almost all countries.
- Inventory cycle: sales-to-inventory ratios rising.
- Housing cycle: OK, building permits led by mortgage-spread compression.
- Credit cycle (listed): fundamentals point to tighter spreads; lending conditions not particularly tightening.
- *"It takes a war to bring down an economy this strong."* And that's what we got.

### Private credit = subprime 2007 echo (calibration disagreement with [[LynAlden]])
- Weakest link. Cockroaches popping up: Cliffwater redemptions; JPM limiting lending to private funds.
- Trigger: AI coding agents eroding SaaS business models → software-loan markdowns → BDC share prices falling.
- **"Contained to subprime" redux:** the reassurance that private credit losses are contained to private credit is naive.
- **Bank lending to NBFIs has "mushroomed" over the last couple of years** — that's the vector of contagion into the listed credit market and then the economy.
- *"Private credit fund within a private credit fund" = CDO-squared redux.* Same structural mistake.
- *"There's nothing new in finance."*

**Note:** This is a meaningful calibration disagreement with [[LynAlden]] (who said bank contagion is limited at 7-8% of total assets, risk-reduced transmission). Same facts, different conclusions. White views the *rate of growth* in bank-NBFI lending as alarming; Alden views the *stock* as manageable.

### Gold's recent weakness
- Recent breakdown in gold as bombs drop. White: probably real yields rising + dollar rally + potential capital reallocation in the Middle East (sovereigns selling what they can to meet other needs).
- But the primary bull trend is intact — "nothing has changed on the structural drivers."

## Contradictions
- **Genuine calibration disagreement with [[LynAlden]] on private credit contagion.** Alden: bank exposure is ~7-8% of total assets, risk-reduced transmission, individual failures possible but not aggregate systemic. White: bank lending to NBFIs has "mushroomed," this is the contagion vector, same "contained to subprime" reassurance as 2007. **Same facts, different conclusions** — this is the wiki's second substantive calibration difference (after Alden's softer take on inflation persistence).
- Not a contradiction but a **meaningful nuance**: White is overall somewhere between Alden's "don't overreact" stance and Paulo's "Anatomy of a Crash" framing. White agrees the US economy is remarkably strong pre-war (echoes Alden) *but* sees private credit as a systemic risk vector (contra Alden, closer to Paulo). A sober synthesis voice.
