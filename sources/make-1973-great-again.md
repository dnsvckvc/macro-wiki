## Summary
[[PauloMacro]]'s 8 March 2026 deep-dive analog study comparing the Iran/US/Israel war to the 1973 Yom Kippur War / Arab Oil Embargo. The current crisis is far worse by any metric (20% of global supply vs. 8% in 1973). Paulo walks through 1973 asset-class charts (crude quadrupled, precious metals doubled, equities −17% by year-end, short yields popped immediately, long yields eventually rose 6.7%→8%+). He then lays out second-order effects at $150/bbl across sectors (petrochems, cement, automotive, food processing, textiles, construction, levered PE roll-ups, retailers, discretionary consumer), argues a severe global recession is "nearly inevitable," and makes the case that **bonds are not a portfolio stabilizer in this kind of recession but an accelerant** because the deficit/GDP will blow out (war spending + collapsing tax receipts). Also identifies a wheat trade (the "geopolitical VIX") and flags food export bans as the social-unrest catalyst. Herbert Hoover's memoirs quoted on capital fleeing "like a loose cannon on the deck of the world."

## Key Claims
- **1973 analog in detail:** crude quadrupled from ~$3 to ~$12 and most of the rally happened *after the ceasefire*. USD rallied, D-Mark sold off. Precious metals initially sold off but doubled by mid-Q1 1974. Equities (S&P peaked Jan 1973 = Nifty Fifty) clung to highs until end-October, then collapsed −17% by year-end. Short yields popped immediately from ~7% to 8%; 10Y hung around for months then rose from 6.7% to 8%+ in 1974. The curve heavily inverted.
- **"A severe global recession is nearly inevitable at this point."** No Nash equilibrium for a deal; US killed Iran's supreme leader ("their pope") and schoolchildren. Iran's optimal strategy is full Houthi: harass/blockade, send US gasoline to $8+, throw the world into recession where allies blame Trump, destroy him in the midterms.
- **Bonds are not a stabilizer — they're an accelerant.** In a jobs recession + energy crisis: tax receipts collapse + war spending skyrockets → deficit/GDP blows out. Term premia in the long end will rise. Middle East sovereign wealth potentially turns seller of foreign assets. This is the "No Fly Zone" scenario.
- **Second-order effects at $150/bbl** (systematically catalogs high-energy-COGS sectors that get destroyed): petrochems/plastics/steel/aluminum, cement/glass, pulp/paper/packaging, smelter-related, miners, automotive/components ("armageddon" per a reader), food processing, textiles/consumer goods (Bangladesh "dead"), construction/real estate, big-box retailers ("Goodnight WMT 40x earnings?"), levered industrial/PE roll-ups, airlines/cruises/hotels.
- **Wheat trade:** wheat is the "geopolitical VIX." In 1973 wheat ripped from ~$2.60 to $5.20 (summer) then another 50% to $6.35 by February. Kuwait already declared a food export ban; more nations will follow. Arab Spring's root cause was food prices. Paulo is long wheat and fertilizer names.

## Contradictions
- **Nuance on bonds (not a contradiction):** Paulo argues the long end is *not* a haven in this kind of recession (deficit/GDP blows out, sovereign sellers emerge, term premia expand). Muir's long-bond trade from [[vix-inversion-buying-bonds-japan-jpm-option-whale]] is concentrated at 2Y/5Y with the long-end only as a "contrarian punt." The two positions are compatible: short-end duration benefits from eventual rate cuts, long-end suffers from the fiscal deterioration Paulo describes. This may be the most important tension in the wiki — and both authors probably agree on the mechanism even if their positioning emphasis differs.
