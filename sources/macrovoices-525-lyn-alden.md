## Summary
MacroVoices Episode 525 (26 March 2026). [[LynAlden]] (founder of Lyn Alden Investment Strategy; author of *Broken Money*) interviewed by Eric Townsend. The wiki's **eighth author** and the first **partial counterpoint** — Alden is bearish overall but pushes back explicitly on several of the wiki's more catastrophist framings. Key contributions: (1) the **multipolar-world framework** (US exiting peak unipolar hyperpower status; this war is a milestone in a Dalio-style empire decline); (2) a sober explanation for **why gold sold off during the war** (extreme pre-war rally removed asymmetry; sovereigns sell liquidity they have, not what they want); (3) **$150 oil is sustainable** (same nominal as 2008, inflation-adjusted much lower); only $200+ is crippling; (4) **inflation persistence depends on money supply growth, not supply shocks** — the spike should fade absent fresh monetary expansion; (5) **private credit contagion into banks is limited** — banks' $1.9T exposure to NBFIs is 7-8% of $25T total assets, risk-reduced; (6) **Warsh vs Powell isn't a big deal** — FOMC has 12 voters, the war has narrowed the difference; (7) **Russia is the conflict winner**; Europe hit hardest; China resilient. Notes Powell's term expires May 15 but he could stay on as board member; Warsh confirmation is "on hold pending criminal investigation of Jay Powell."

## Key Claims

### The multipolar-world framework
- US is exiting peak unipolar hyperpower status (peaked late 1990s). Gradual shift toward multipolar since. China is the main counter-pole; India and Brazil are secondary; Europe's pole has *shrunk*.
- Ray Dalio's empire-metrics analysis: education quality is a leading indicator both up and down; global reserve currency is one of the last things to decline (network effects).
- The Iran war is a "milestone" in this shift. "Empires rarely give up their projection capabilities easily, even when the right strategic thing would be to right-size from a position of strength."

### Why precious metals sold off during the war
Multiple factors:
- **Asymmetry was gone:** extreme pre-war rally (gold/silver/platinum) hit Alden's multi-year price targets. No longer "asymmetrically undervalued."
- **Sovereigns sell what they can, not what they want.** Gold is a source of liquidity for many market participants, including potentially sovereign participants in this crisis.
- **Bitcoin held up because it was already washed out** — had opposite pre-war price action (underperformed for months). Fast money was already out; coins held by strong hands. The "portable scarce money" premium matters in crises where capital needs to move across jurisdictions.
- Not calling a permanent top — just noting asymmetry has shifted. Could sell off further OR continue higher; she's no longer high-conviction bull.

### Oil thresholds
- **$150 oil = painful but sustainable.** Same nominal as 2008, but inflation-adjusted much lower (money supply has doubled+; house prices and incomes bigger).
- **$200+ oil = approaching inflation-adjusted highs → crippling.** "Things start breaking down."
- Scenario: multiple shut-ins + severely damaged energy infrastructure + another month+ of near-closed Strait → $200+ "quite possible, if not probable."

### K-shaped economy
- **AI capex + fiscal deficit spending** (healthcare, social security, defense) + older/wealthier Americans are doing fine.
- **New college grads, home buyers, lower-income are struggling.** Non-farm payrolls flat for the better part of a year. AI optimization cutting white-collar jobs.
- Energy price spike "puts pressure at a time when the shields are kind of down."
- **Egypt concrete example:** natural gas import bill tripled ($500M → $1.5B/month); rolling power issues; businesses curfewed after 9pm. Egyptian pound 47→52 per USD.

### Inflation persistence
- **Key principle:** supply shocks are transitory; money supply growth is permanent.
- 2020-21 had permanent inflation because $ supply spiked (QE + direct fiscal injections).
- 2022 energy spike was *less* persistent than it looked because money supply wasn't expanding correspondingly.
- **Current spike should mostly fade** absent fresh monetary expansion. But individual pockets (fertilizer, farmers) may have permanent damage, and weaker EMs *will* lock in higher inflation because they'll print to subsidize.

### The Fed: Warsh ≈ Powell right now
- Powell's term as chair expires **May 15**; could remain on the board (may increase those odds based on recent comments).
- Warsh confirmation "on hold pending criminal investigation of Jay Powell" — "what a crazy world we live in."
- FOMC has 12 voting members; chairman sets tone but isn't a dictator.
- In normal times Warsh would be slightly more dovish and slightly less balance-sheet-oriented; the war has **narrowed that difference** because no credible Fed chair can cut into rising oil inflation.
- Balance-sheet proposals are "largely liquidity-neutral, not that big overall" (partial agreement with [[MichaelHowell]]'s critique but less catastrophist).
- **Any Fed chair steps in on acute liquidity stress** (standing facilities, purchases). That doesn't change.

### Partial counterpoint: private credit contagion is limited
Alden's most notable disagreement with the wiki's baseline:
- Banks have lent ~**$1.9T to NBFIs** — ~**7-8% of total $25T bank assets**.
- The risk is *further* reduced because PC funds must absorb massive losses before it hits the banks that lent to them.
- Individual bank failures possible but aggregate US banking system is not nearly as vulnerable as 2007-08.
- She's "less worried about contagion risks" than most — notes Google Trends for "private credit" is at peak-2008-subprime levels, suggesting some of this is already crowded-bear territory.
- Caveat: high rates from stagflation can pop bubbles (as energy spikes in 2007 helped pop subprime) — so not zero risk, but different transmission.

### Russia is the winner
- Higher energy prices, less sanction pressure, major fertilizer producer → positive for Russia.
- Europe hit hardest. China resilient (flexible, higher pain tolerance).
- Caveat: Iran's Russian/Chinese-supplied equipment "has not necessarily operated as well as they would have hoped" — bad advertising for Russian/Chinese arms exports.

### Food inflation as the 2nd wave
- Agrees with the wiki's Wheat/fertilizer thesis: "Food inflation is one of the most damaging types of inflation you can get."
- In developing countries, food/energy shortages cause revolutions.
- Farmers currently squeezed: higher fertilizer expenses, not yet higher crop prices.

## Key Quotes
> "We're kind of falling back toward a world that historically is more usual, which is that you have multiple poles of power in competition with each other."

> "In times of crisis, sometimes entities have to sell what they can, not what they want to."

> "Just because you have $150 oil doesn't mean the economy can't function. It just means changes have to occur."

> "Supply shocks should mostly go back to baseline unless we get money supply growth accompanying them."

> "I'm less worried about contagion risks [from private credit]."

## Contradictions
- **The wiki's first partial counterpoint.** Alden is broadly bearish (war is a milestone event; Russia wins; Europe loses; food inflation coming; economy struggling on the wrong side of K) but explicitly *less alarmist* than the wiki's baseline on three fronts:
  1. **$150 oil is sustainable** — contrasts with Paulo's framing that "Never Again" requires $200+ crude, $8 gasoline, semi-fab force majeures, etc. (both authors agree $200+ is crippling; they differ on where the "painful but functional" upper bound sits).
  2. **Inflation persistence is contingent on money supply growth.** The current oil-driven spike "should mostly go back to baseline" absent fresh monetary expansion. This is a meaningful contrast with Bianco's "3-ish percent permanent inflation world" and Muir's "inflationary pressures should not be expected to fade."
  3. **Private credit bank contagion is limited.** Banks' exposure is only 7-8% of total assets and is risk-reduced. This contrasts with Paulo's more alarmist framing in [[intensely-concerned-for-risk-with-a-catch]] (software/PC boomerang) and Howell's [[DebtLiquidityRatio]] structural case, though Alden concedes individual bank failures are possible.
- Not contradictions but **calibration differences** — Alden is the wiki's voice for "yes, bearish, but let's not get carried away."
