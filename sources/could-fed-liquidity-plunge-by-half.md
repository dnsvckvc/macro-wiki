## Summary
[[MichaelHowell]]'s 1 April 2026 note debunking a recent Fed research paper (co-authored by [[StephenMiran]]) proposing to reduce the Fed's balance sheet by **$1.2–2.1T (mid-point $1.7T)** while maintaining "ample reserves." Howell argues this would cut "Fed Liquidity" to ~$1.5T — **roughly half** its recent level — and given the historically tight relationship between Fed Liquidity and the S&P 500, would "plainly spell disaster for asset prices." The paper is a "technical ideal rooted in nostalgia for a smaller Fed footprint" that ignores market reality: the coordination problem between the Fed and Treasury, the volatility of private-sector liquidity demand, and the **inelasticity** of the reserve demand curve at low levels (small shifts → big rate swings). Introduces the elegant formulation: **liquidity = assets / duration**, and the concept of **"Treasury QE"** replacing "Fed QE."

## Key Claims
- **Liquidity = assets / duration.** The Fed can't increase net private-sector assets, only change average duration via maturity transformation. The Treasury *can* do both (increase the asset pool via debt issuance and change duration via bill/note mix). This ties the two policymakers together.
- **If the Fed shrinks its balance sheet but the Treasury doesn't offset** (via more bills or TGA drawdown), the net liquidity impact is unambiguously negative. The Fed paper *assumes* someone else will increase liquidity in an offsetting move — but this coordination "rarely exists in practice."
- **"Treasury QE" replacing "Fed QE":** the policy shift where Treasury bills replace Fed reserves as the primary liquidity instrument. This satisfies the administration's desire to reduce the Fed's influence. But it requires explicit coordination.
- **The demand curve for reserves becomes more inelastic at lower levels.** The Fed paper assumes the demand curve simply shifts left; Howell argues that the new equilibrium sits on a far more inelastic segment → small demand/supply shifts cause **big swings in market interest rates.**
- **The paper ignores precautionary demand for liquidity** and the volatility of private-sector demand. It also only addresses bank reserves, not the full private-sector dollar liquidity needs (which include global demand given USD dominance).
- **The S&P 500 / Fed Liquidity chart** shows Fed Liquidity at the proposed ~$1.5T threshold would correspond to a dramatically lower equity market.

## Key Quotes
> "Shrinking the Fed's balance sheet is not a neutral policy."

> "Liquidity = assets / duration"

> "It's a risky plan that could lead to greater volatility, notably in the important Treasury market, and so ultimately result in higher borrowing costs."

> "The Fed could lose its ability to easily control rates, ultimately forcing it to choose between abandoning balance sheet targets entirely or else risking its operational credibility."
