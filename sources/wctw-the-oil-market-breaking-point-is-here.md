## Summary
[[HFIResearch]]'s 20 April 2026 follow-up to [[wctw-the-oil-market-breaking-point]] (26 March 2026) — a **public** piece (HFI's earlier Breaking Point piece was paywalled) that formally declares the mid-April threshold crossed. Brent at $95 the day of publication; HFI argues that level is disconnected from physical reality and that the world faces a choice among crude storage draws, product storage draws, and demand destruction — *"This is just math."* The piece also contains a **consequential empirical update**: HFI directly walks back the insurance-closure thesis (which he had held early in the conflict) after observing IRGC physically threatening tankers with gunfire over the weekend of 18–19 April, producing the first mass-turn-around of tankers since HFI began tracking. Cites [[PauloMacro]]'s recommendation of [[RobertPape]] and endorses Pape's *Why the Ceasefire Keeps Failing* — the **first explicit cross-author citation from HFI** (previously HFI data flowed to Paulo; now Paulo's frameworks flow to HFI).

## Key Claims

### The breaking point has been crossed
- *"In our write-up published on March 25, we outlined a range of scenarios and explained that the oil market's breaking point would be mid-April. We are now past the breaking point."*
- From here, **11–13mmbpd of supply outage** must manifest as (1) crude storage draws, (2) product storage draws, or (3) demand destruction.
- The breaking point "coincides with the last barrels from the Persian Gulf to the end user" — i.e., the moment floating inventory in transit has fully landed and onshore stocks must start drawing.

### The positive-feedback cycle
HFI lays out the mechanical cycle the market is now entering:
> Elevated crude prices > compressed refining margins > lower refined products > lower product storage > higher refining margins > higher throughput > higher crude prices

- Global refinery outage currently **~5mmbpd**, with ~3mmbpd concentrated in the Middle East.
- Asia and Europe are reducing refinery throughput — but lower throughput ≠ lower end-user demand, just lower product-inventory builds.
- This is the *physical-market mirror* of Paulo's [[CrackSpreads]] rollover argument in [[crack-spreads-are-the-tell]]: the compression of refining margins *feeds back into* higher crude by forcing product drawdowns. Both authors describe the same regime from different starting points.

### The storage math (one-page summary)
- Cumulative storage lost (from HFI's 25 March Breaking Point piece): **~1bn bbls by 25 March** → ~**1.2bn bbls** by end-April → **~1.59bn bbls** by end-May → **~1.98bn bbls** by end-June.
- By first week of May: **only Japan and China** among Asian countries will have real excess crude inventory. Everyone else scrambles.
- By same timeframe, Europe feels the lack of crude. US crude exports averaging ~**5.5mmbpd** by then.
- OECD inventories at operational minimum; remaining excess sits in the US.
- **US commercial crude storage below ~400mmbbl, approaching operational minimum (370–380mmbbl) by end of July** — this includes an estimated cumulative US SPR release of **139mmbbl**.

### The post-peace normalization math
Even with a long-lasting peace deal starting next week:
- **~160mmbbl of floating storage** in tankers — will take 30–40 days to transit + offload, plus 20 days to return.
- **70 VLCCs headed for the US** right now for US crude to Asia — 6–8 weeks to load + 45–50 days transit + 20–25 days offload-and-return = **3 months minimum** before meaningful tanker traffic returns to Hormuz.
- To alleviate Middle East onshore build, need **~100 VLCCs**. 600mmbbl of onshore storage; need ~200mmbbl drawdown cushion before producers restart.
- Physically impossible to renormalize before **mid-to-late June**, at earliest.
- Only once Middle East onshore drains to sufficient cushion can Saudi/UAE/Kuwait/Qatar/Iraq/Bahrain restart — another few-week process beyond that.

### The 139mmbbl US SPR release figure
HFI's first published total-cumulative SPR release number: **~139mmbbl from the US** is already assumed in the end-of-July model. Matches Paulo's structural ceiling argument in [[making-macro-great-again-quick-updates]] that the US can't go much further without violating salt-cavern structural limits.

### The export-ban cascade
HFI projects the Trump administration will:
1. **Ban US product exports first.**
2. If US refineries throttle throughput due to compressed margins, **ban crude exports second**.
> *"Which would be a disastrous scenario for US shale and Canadian oil producers (more on this in a later write-up)."*

This is a new specific US-policy-response forecast not previously in the wiki.

### The IRGC-gunfire finding (consequential update)
> *"IRGC physically implemented the closure by threatening tankers with gunfire. We saw this visibly through tanker activity. This is the first time since we started tracking tanker activity that we saw tankers turn around in volume. In the past, we saw maybe 1 or 2 tankers do that, but not in the size it did this weekend."*

HFI then explicitly walks back his own prior stance:
> *"There were theories that the Strait of Hormuz was closed due to a lack of insurance. I also shared that view in the early stages of the conflict, but as the events progressed, especially what transpired this weekend, I was shocked."*

Implications:
- IRGC is **firmly in physical control** of Hormuz. Not insurance-mediated.
- *"the conflict will need to get worse before it gets better."*
- **Directly updates [[DrAnasAlHajji]]'s insurance-closure thesis.** The two mechanisms are not mutually exclusive — insurance costs still matter — but HFI's 18–19 April weekend observation is the first eye-witness confirmation of *primary* physical causation. Al-Hajji's thesis explained the *first weeks*; HFI's new data covers the late-April phase.

### Pape and the ceasefire
HFI endorses Paulo's recommendation of [[RobertPape]] and flags Pape's recent piece *"Why the Ceasefire Keeps Failing."* This is the **first explicit cross-author citation from HFI** in the wiki. The prior direction of reference flow was Paulo → HFI (for data); now Paulo → HFI for analytical framework.

### The price-target regime break
- If Hormuz opens by end-April → HFI's prior forecast: Brent "drops down" to **$110** (trading at $95 on 20 April).
- If Hormuz stays closed past April → **no price forecast possible.** *"This will have been the largest oil supply outage in the history of the oil market by a magnitude of 4x. Fundamental market theories will no longer apply here because there's no price for outright shortages. When a market runs out of fuel, it just runs out."*
- Demand destruction required = **COVID-lockdown-magnitude** to rebalance 11–13mmbpd of supply outage.
- Even that only *prevents further deterioration*, doesn't produce a surplus.

## Contradictions
- **Al-Hajji insurance-closure thesis:** HFI had initially shared this view (per [[macrovoices-526-dr-anas-al-hajji]]'s framework); now explicitly walks it back based on 18–19 April eye-witness IRGC gunfire. The two theses aren't mutually exclusive — insurance costs matter — but HFI's new data reverses the *primary-cause* attribution.
- **HFI's own scenario-3 Brent target:** March 26 piece said "if Hormuz stays closed end-April → Brent $110–$120." April 20 piece says "no price forecast possible" past April. HFI walks back his own pre-specified price path in the scenario that is now live, explicitly crediting the unprecedented 4x-magnitude-vs-any-prior-crisis as the reason fundamental theory breaks down. Not a contradiction — an extension: HFI's original scenario 3 assumed prompt post-April resolution; this piece addresses the prolonged-closure case.
- **Price-level vs reality:** Brent at $95 on 20 April; HFI says this price is disconnected from physical reality. This echoes Rozencwajg's 9 April "forward curve hasn't repriced" and [[macrovoices-528-rory-johnston]]'s 16 April $32 Dated-Brent-vs-futures spread observation. Three authors, same conclusion, different entry points.
