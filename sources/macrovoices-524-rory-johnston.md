## Summary
MacroVoices Episode 524 (19 March 2026) — **the earliest MacroVoices transcript in the wiki**, about 3 weeks into the war. [[RoryJohnston]] (Commodity Context founder, oil-market analyst) interviewed by Eric Townsend. The wiki's **tenth author** and second dedicated physical-oil specialist (complementing [[AdamRozencwajg]]). Johnston opens with a candid admission: he and Al-Hajji both thought the Strait closure was unrealistic. "I never thought a US president would engage in a war with Iran without a plan." Key contributions: (1) the **Trump administration had no plan** (IEA SPR release came 2 weeks late; marine insurance facility improvised); (2) the **Venezuela / Delcy Rodriguez template** that Trump expected to replay in Iran — and why it failed; (3) historical contrast with the **1980s tanker wars** (450 ships attacked, 55 sunk — flow NEVER halted); this time: **90-95% transit reduction**; (4) **insurance math alone doesn't explain it** — war premium at 5% of vessel value, but arbs easily cover; ships aren't moving because traders expect Trump to TACO tomorrow; (5) **3 months minimum to renormalize** even if it ends today; (6) $200 crude is "bare minimum" for year-plus; demand destruction needed equals peak-COVID; (7) **Trump goal-shifting** — from "don't panic" to "high oil is good for US"; (8) **export-controls risk** — Jones Act waiver as precursor.

## Key Claims

### Trump admin had no plan
- IEA coordinated SPR release came **2 weeks after** the war started. "If you were planning this, you would have an IEA release lined up. We saw that ahead of the Gulf War."
- Bessent's Treasury marine insurance facility was clearly improvised.
- No advance SPR refill work.
- *"It just seems insane that we entered this without a plan."*

### The Venezuela template that failed
- Trump's playbook: massive military buildup → quick regime flip → declare victory.
- **Worked for Venezuela** (January 2026): Trump "kidnapped Nicolas Maduro and his wife" Saturday morning; Delcy Rodriguez in as interim president by Monday making a deal.
- **Expected to replay for Iran:** the identical military buildup pattern; Trump probably expected to be rolling over Cuba by now.
- **Failed because Iranian political culture ≠ Venezuelan political culture.** "Arguably the biggest miscalculation here from the White House." No Delcy Rodriguez character emerged in Iran. No opportunity to declare victory.
- June 2025 Iran episode (14 bunker-buster bombs on Fordow/Natanz/Isfahan) set the template expectation — symbolic Iranian retaliation, Trump declares ceasefire, crude ends day down $10. Didn't happen this time.

### Historical contrast: 1980s tanker wars
- **450 ships attacked** during the Iran-Iraq tanker wars. 250 tankers hit. **55 tankers sunk or scuttled.**
- **Flow NEVER halted** despite far more damage than now.
- This time: **90-95% structural reduction** in Strait transit.

### Why insurance math alone doesn't explain it
- War insurance premium: pre-war **0.25%** of vessel value → now **5%** (per Bloomberg).
- That's a **$5M premium on a $100M vessel** — massive increase.
- **But the arbitrage easily clears it:** Dubai physical crude >$150/bbl vs. lower on the "bad" side; practically negative on the "bad" side.
- **Ships still aren't moving.** Johnston's best explanation: *"The base case expectation is that Trump backs out here, another TACO. Why spend $5M and risk your ship if this could be over tomorrow?"*

### Even if it ends today
- **3 months minimum to renormalize** even with instant resumption.
- **340-400mmbbl "air pocket"** in normal Middle East → Asia flow already created; hasn't yet hit land because laden tankers are still in transit.
- When the air pocket hits Asia in 3-4 weeks: **10-15+ mmbpd inventory draw** — never happened before.
- Asian refineries already reducing operating rates, cutting product output, front-running the shortage.

### The scale of what's needed
- To balance the market if 15-20mmbpd stays offline: **demand destruction equivalent to peak-COVID lockdowns** but via *price alone* with no pandemic.
- $200 Brent is "bare minimum" for a year-plus scenario.
- **Where the pain falls:** wealthy countries = "debilitating, recessionary, nigh-depressionary price shock." Poor EMs in the Global South = *shortages* (outbid by wealthy nations). Pakistan LNG 2022 precedent: broke committed contract, paid breakage fee, shipped gas to Europe for the arb.

### Trump goal-shifting
- Week 1-2: "Don't panic, this will end soon. Low oil is my brand."
- Week 3: "Actually, high oil prices are good for the US because we're the largest producer." (Tweet.)
- Rory: *"Low oil president has been his brand since the 1980s. I don't know how much he can really buy this."*
- Most worrying development: **the oil-price feedback mechanism is losing its sensitivity on Trump.** He's now looking for a way to declare victory — but the Iranian regime is entrenching instead of flipping.
- Israeli freelancing: South Pars gas field attack (first upstream hit) was "against the wishes of the White House." Israel wants *more* escalation.

### Benchmark divergence explained
- Brent ~$110, WTI ~$119 (max so far, not above $120). But Oman $185. Jet fuel Singapore $200+.
- These aren't just logistics noise — they're **the leading edge of what's coming for Brent and WTI.**
- Asian refiners are **starting to bid into the Brent basket** (Bloomberg reported) as they realize this isn't ending tomorrow.
- WTI underperforms because it's "the furthest grade away" — months to get from Cushing to the coast to the Middle East to Asia. "People need these barrels today."
- **$35 intraday spread in Brent** two Mondays ago (Trump jawbone whipsaw) — "I don't believe has ever happened before." Many traders lost their jobs. Futures now less willing to front-run physical tightness.

### Export-controls risk
- Biden admin mused this in 2022 — banning/restricting refined product exports from the US.
- Trump is laying the framework. **Jones Act waiver** (temporary, recent) is the precursor — enables moving US Gulf Coast crude/diesel around without relying on Jones-Act-compliant tankers, which is necessary to avoid backlogs if exports are banned.
- Short-term: attractive for the White House (lowers US prices).
- Long-term: "Short-circuits wealthy markets' capacity to pass this on through price. We would likely end up facing physical shortages in advanced markets."

### Fertilizer and food
- Gulf = ~**1/3 of global fertilizer supply**, vast majority of global **helium** supply.
- Crop yields decline, food production declines, shipping costs soar. 1970s parallel *actually undersells* this: 70s supply losses were small; the big thing was logistics. This time: acute supply losses AND logistics.

## Contradictions
- None. **Tenth-party confirmation.** Johnston is an oil-market specialist who confirms and deepens key wiki claims:
  - **Al-Hajji's insurance thesis is inadequate alone** — Johnston agrees the insurance math is real (5% premium) but notes the arbitrage easily clears it, so something else is keeping ships in port. His answer (TACO expectation) complements Al-Hajji's (coordinated US insurance pressure) rather than contradicting it. Both explanations may be partially true.
  - **Paulo's Nash thesis is operationally confirmed.** Trump expected the Venezuela template (regime flip → victory); Iran's political culture made that impossible. This is the operational reason Nash holds — not just that Iran *won't* back down but that there's no internal-Iran mechanism for the Trump administration's expected flip.
  - **Rozencwajg's "forward curve hasn't repriced" thesis gets a market-microstructure explanation** — the $35 intraday Brent spread blew traders out, making futures markets less willing to front-run physical tightness. Traders are "hopium-bound" to the TACO narrative.
