# Commodity Supercycle

A **decade(+)-long structural bull market across the broad commodity complex**, driven by an underlying demand regime change that supply cannot meet without sustained higher prices. Distinguished from cyclical commodity bull markets by duration, breadth, and the persistence of the supply-side constraint.

[[OleHansen]] in [[macrovoices-529-ole-hansen]] identifies three completed/ongoing supercycles:

1. **Post-WWII reconstruction** (~1945–1955). Industrial rebuild driving steel, copper, base materials.
2. **1970s inflation** (~1970–1980). OPEC oil shock + general inflation; the canonical strong correlation between commodity supercycle and broad inflation.
3. **2000s China industrialization** (~2000–2011). Urbanization-driven base-metals + bulk-commodities boom.

## The ongoing fourth wave (2020–?)

Hansen calls this **the energy-transition supercycle.** Five years in (from the 2020 pandemic low), Bloomberg Commodity Index up ~160% from the trough. *"We are heading higher simply because we are still in a power-hungry world… we want to conduct all that power, and that is just very commodity intensive."*

The driver is asymmetric:
- **Demand:** electrification + AI compute + reshoring + multi-polar inventory rebuild ([[JustInTimeVsJustInCase]]) all pull simultaneously on copper, aluminium, steel, fertilizer, energy.
- **Supply:** the *"old world is striking back against the new world"* ([[JeffCurrie]] via Hansen) — legacy producers cannot build new mines, refineries, or fields fast enough, especially under permitting + ESG + capital-discipline regimes.

## Why high prices may not cure high prices this cycle

Hansen: *"The best cure for high price is a high price… that is still obviously relevant in many areas. The most striking one recently has been cocoa prices… But if you look at both the energy and the metal space, what we're missing or could be missing in such a scenario is simply the supply side not being able to respond to higher prices."*

Counter-example: [[Cocoa]] (2,500 → 12,000 → utterly collapsed back) — both demand and supply responded. *Not* what's expected for energy and metals.

## Leg sequencing (Hansen, April 2026)

| Driver | When |
|---|---|
| Metals | Last year (2025) |
| Energy | Start of 2026 |
| **Agriculture** | **2H 2026 → 2027** (next leg) |

This forward-sequence placement of agriculture is one of Hansen's load-bearing predictions — agriculture sector total return is only +1.3% one-year / +5% two-year as of April 2026, suggesting the leg is *ahead*, not behind.
