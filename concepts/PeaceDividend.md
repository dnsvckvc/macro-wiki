# Peace Dividend

The bull case for the Iran/US/Israel war's *resolution* — the argument that removing the Iranian regime's capacity to back "bad proxy actors" in the Middle East enables a multi-decade period of regional stability and prosperity, and that this future is bullish for risk assets even accounting for the war's acute damage.

## The Dimon articulation
[[JamieDimon]] in an interview cited by [[KevinMuir]] in [[making-sense-of-the-last-two-months]]:
> *"I do think there is more geopolitical risk than we have seen since World War II. Can it play out in a way that's good? I think in the Middle East, it's possible they end with a situation with a higher chance of long-term peace. Because [[SaudiArabia]] wants it. [[UAE]] wants it. America wants it. Israel wants it. And we've destroyed — for the most part — the really bad proxy actors there."*

Muir's 19 April retrospective concedes the exuberant rally "is at least getting partially priced in" to this scenario — the S&P has pushed back *above* its pre-war close (6879 → 7126).

## Where it sits in the wiki debate
The Peace Dividend is **the institutional-bull opposite** of [[PauloMacro]]'s [[NorthStar]] framework:
- **Peace Dividend:** 50-year Middle East stabilization justifies re-rating risk above pre-war levels; ceasefire → deal → growth.
- **North Star:** even a deal is bearish because Iran will only sign if handed Hormuz, which locks in [[SaudiArabia]]/[[UAE]] Nash shut-in indefinitely (see [[oil-and-retail]]).

**Counterweight evidence already in the wiki:**
- [[MichaelEvery]] ([[macrovoices-525-michael-every]]) treats the "bad proxies removed" framing as part of the **media-narrative warfare** layer of the fog of war, not as a primary-source strategic fact.
- [[DrAnasAlHajji]] ([[macrovoices-526-dr-anas-al-hajji]]): **every country post-crisis links energy to national security** — a structural shift that is bearish for aggregate efficiency and bullish for oil prices regardless of which "side" wins.
- [[LukeGromen]] ([[macrovoices-528-luke-gromen]]): Suez 1956 as base case — the *US*, not Iran, is the hegemon whose position has structurally weakened.
- [[JimBianco]] ([[macrovoices-527-jim-bianco]]): **pre-Feb-28 market levels are permanently gone** due to embedded risk premia; even with a deal, the economy doesn't return to pre-war.

## Why it's a useful wiki concept
The Peace Dividend is the clearest articulation of what the April rally *is pricing in*, even if no wiki author endorses it. Knowing the bull thesis explicitly sharpens the bear thesis and clarifies what new evidence would have to arrive (a durable deal + Saudi resuming exports + oil normalizing) to justify the current level.

## Muir's 20 April steel-man and rebuttal ([[outlining-the-bull-and-bear-arguments]])
In his part-2 bull/bear piece, Muir:
- **Steel-mans** the Peace Dividend alongside the [[MarkoPapic]] [[FadeGeopoliticalAngst]] framework: the "buy the geopolitical angst trade worked in spades" even if the dip was smaller than expected.
- **Rebuts via second-order effects:** even granting the long-term-peace framing, the war itself teaches every country that it needs to increase military (especially navy) spending permanently. This means less money for productive assets; government crowding out private investment; financial repression abroad (Korea Dec 24 tax rebate, Colombia pension rules); and reversal of the globalization premium that America has "exploited better than anyone."
- Net: the Peace Dividend partial-priced-in understates both the direct-cost tail of the war (Johnston/Rozencwajg physical dislocation) *and* the structural reversal of the globalization-funded US asset premium (Muir's flow-of-funds bear argument).
