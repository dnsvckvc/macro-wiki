# Cotton

Soft agricultural fiber commodity. The wiki's canonical example of **a non-energy commodity whose price is set primarily by the energy market** through substitution dynamics.

## The oil-to-cotton chain ([[OleHansen]] in [[macrovoices-529-ole-hansen]])

1. **Cotton's main competitor is synthetic fiber.**
2. **Synthetic fiber is a petrochemical-derived product** — its production cost rises directly with crude / natural gas / refined-product prices.
3. **High oil prices → synthetic fiber more expensive → demand substitutes back into cotton → cotton price rises.**

A complementary force in 2026: drought conditions in US winter wheat regions (which overlap with cotton-growing areas) constrain cotton supply on top of the demand-side substitution.

Hansen: *"What is the competition for cotton? That's in synthetic fiber. That's a petrochemical-derived product. And that basically, that's where you have the link… If synthetic fiber goes up, well, then you can return to the real deal, to the real cotton."*

## The broader fuel-to-soft-commodity substitution family

Hansen identifies cotton as one example of a class of **energy-driven soft-commodity trades**. Others mentioned:

| Commodity | Substitution mechanism |
|---|---|
| **Cotton** | Petrochemical synthetic fiber → real cotton |
| **Sugar** | Brazilian sugar cane → ethanol (biofuel) competes with sugar production |
| **Soybean oil** | Biofuel demand pulls vegetable oil → already in backwardation |
| **Corn** | US ethanol mandate links corn to fuel demand |

In all cases the *direction* of cause is unintuitive: oil moves first; the soft commodity follows. This is one mechanism by which an energy supply shock spreads into food prices and inflation more broadly.
