# Fed Look-Through (Energy Price Spikes)

The Federal Reserve's **historical pattern of NOT raising rates in response to oil-supply-shock-driven energy price spikes** — and often *cutting* into the spike — on the reasoning that:

1. Higher rates do not encourage an energy-supply response (you can't drill faster with tighter money).
2. An energy-supply shock acts like a *tax hike* on the consumer — it is contractionary, not stimulative.
3. Headline inflation from energy will eventually moderate as prices level off.
4. Core inflation (ex food and energy) is what monetary policy should target.

[[KevinMuir]] formalizes the wiki's case study in [[why-did-the-front-end-get-hammered]] (27 April 2026).

## The canonical 2007–2008 case

Oil rose from **$55 to $145** between mid-2007 and mid-2008. Across that entire move, the Fed *cut rates* — Fed Funds went from 5.25% in August 2007 to 2.00% by April 2008.

### FOMC minutes excerpts ([[KevinMuir]] in [[why-did-the-front-end-get-hammered]])

**Jan 29–30, 2008 — 50bp cut:**
> *"Most members agreed that a 50 basis point easing at this meeting would likely not contribute to an increase in inflation pressures given the actual and expected weakness in economic growth and the consequent reduction in pressures on resources."*
> *"A leveling-off of energy and commodity prices such as that embedded in futures markets would also help moderate inflation pressures."*

**March 18, 2008 — 75bp cut (one of the largest on record), oil running higher:**
The dissents (Fisher and Plosser) argued for less aggressive easing on inflation grounds, citing rising inflation expectations and international price pressures. The majority cut anyway, citing financial-market stress. *"Both Messrs. Fisher and Plosser were concerned that inflation expectations could potentially become unhinged should the Committee continue to lower the funds rate in the current environment."* The dissent was overridden.

**June 24–25, 2008 — held at 2.00%, oil at $145:**
> *"Headline inflation was generally expected to moderate in coming quarters, reflecting importantly an anticipated leveling-out of prices for energy and other commodities."*
> *"A number of participants noted that the outlook for slack in resource utilization should tend to limit the extent of pass-through, contain the degree of inflation spillover."*

Even at the absolute peak in oil — and the absolute peak of the energy-driven inflation pressure — the Fed *would not raise rates*. They held.

[[KevinMuir]]: *"Time and time again, the Fed chose to find excuses to cut rates even as oil was soaring."*

## Why the 2026 Fed broke from this pattern

The Q1 2026 Fed has been *uncharacteristically hawkish* — moving through the 28 Feb war by withdrawing dovish dissents (Waller/Bowman at March 17–18 FOMC) and confirming an on-hold stance. This is contrary to the [[FedLookThrough]] historical pattern.

[[KevinMuir]]'s diagnosis ([[why-did-the-front-end-get-hammered]]):
- **Not political (DOJ Powell investigation):** *"Powell has a strong sense of duty, and I am convinced he is always doing what he believes to be in the country's best interest."*
- **The 2022/2023 inflation flak:** [[ScottBessent]] and [[StephenMiran]] were *"brutal in their complaints that the FOMC let inflation expectations rise too quickly."*
- **The "fight the last war" instinct:** central bankers are over-correcting against the prior cycle's perceived error. The April 2026 Fed is fighting 2022, not running the 2008 playbook.

## Why look-through will likely return — Muir's forecast

With Dec 2026 SOFR already at 3.875% (3 cuts removed + small hike chance), the next crude spike has nowhere to go in the *removal-of-easing* channel — the implied path implies *actual hikes*. Per [[RemovalOfEasingVsHikes]], that is a much harder political action.

Muir: *"If we get this scenario, just like in 2007-2008, the Federal Reserve will come up with tons of excuses why they don't need to tighten into an energy supply shock. They will talk about core inflation. They will explain the downside risks to growth. They will intellectually contort themselves in any way possible to avoid raising rates."*

The market is starting to price this — the last two weeks of crude vs SOFR regression show yellow dots *below* the line (rates back to pre-FOMC levels for given crude).
