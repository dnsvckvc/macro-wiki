# Swap Spreads

The difference between the fixed rate on an interest-rate swap and the yield of a government bond of the same maturity (SOFR swap minus cash Treasury). The spread captures the relative pricing, credit, funding, and liquidity conditions between swaps and cash bonds.

Structurally negative in the post-2008 regime (reflecting bank balance-sheet and SLR costs to go on swap vs. cash), but the *magnitude* of the negativity is informative.

## Why Paulo tracks them ([[the-anatomy-of-a-crash]])
A **more deeply negative** swap spread means Treasuries are cheap vs swaps. [[PauloMacro]] enumerates three non-exclusive drivers:
1. Heavy Treasury supply or selling pressure cheapens cash bonds when private balance sheets are reluctant to absorb.
2. Strong fixed-receive demand in swaps (less likely when swaps are *rising*).
3. Binding balance sheet, capital, and SLR constraints making the basis arbitrage capital-intensive; hedge funds need a much more negative spread to justify using balance sheet.

A combination of (1) and (3) leads basis traders to degross: sell cash bonds and cover futures shorts — the precise symptom observed in the March 2026 tape.

## Signal (March 2026)
10Y and 30Y swap spreads deeply negative and approaching Liberation Day levels — confirming strain in the [[BasisTrade]] even while overnight rate plumbing still looks normal. (Per [[the-anatomy-of-a-crash]], 14 March 2026.)

As of 12 March 2026 ([[making-macro-great-again-quick-updates]]), Paulo notes the deepening in longer-dated swap spreads is "telling us there is a problem — maybe with the basis trade and guys needing to cut books." He links this to why **cash bonds are weakening** despite the recessionary macro backdrop — the arbitrage is unwinding and dealers are selling cash Treasuries.
