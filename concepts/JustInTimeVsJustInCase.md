# Just-in-Time vs. Just-in-Case

[[JeffCurrie]]'s frame for the post-2020 supply-chain regime change, used by [[OleHansen]] in [[macrovoices-529-ole-hansen]] to explain a structural lift in commodity-demand baseline.

**Just-in-time** (1990s–2019). Inventory minimization optimized for cost. Reliance on stable global trade + frictionless logistics + low geopolitical premium. Producers and consumers alike held minimal stock.

**Just-in-case** (2020–). Inventory *insurance* prioritized over cost. Driven by the cumulative experience of:
- COVID supply-chain seizures (2020–2022)
- Russia/Ukraine commodity-flow disruptions (2022–)
- US-China decoupling and tariffs
- The 2026 Iran/US/Israel war and [[StraitOfHormuz]] closure
- General "breakdown in normal relations" between major economies

Hansen: *"We are much more focused on having ample supplies instead of just having enough supplies and being reliant on supply chains being able to deliver, so you don't run into any shortages. And that basically means that there will be a demand — that it will drive demand — because inventory levels need to be kept around the world at higher levels than it was in the past."*

## Why it matters for the [[CommoditySupercycle]]

The framework explains a **persistent step-up in commodity demand independent of GDP**: the world rebuilds inventories simultaneously across virtually every economy and every commodity category. Even sideways industrial growth would imply rising commodity demand under just-in-case logistics.

This is structurally bullish for any commodity that can be stockpiled (oil, refined products, fertilizer, grains, base metals, sulfuric acid) and is **disinflationary in producer pricing only when supply chains return to JIT confidence** — which the Iran war, by extending the cumulative supply-shock memory, now further postpones.
