# Price-to-Free-Cash-Flow (P/FCF)

Valuation metric: market cap divided by free cash flow. Generally considered harder to manipulate than P/E because it reflects cash actually available after capex, rather than accounting earnings.

## Why it's load-bearing in [[what-not-to-buy]]
[[KevinMuir]] uses P/FCF to flip the standard "MAG7 is cheap" narrative:
- On NTM P/E, [[MAG7]] looks historically cheap (~22.5x).
- On P/FCF, MAG7 has **never been more expensive** in the post-COVID era.
- The divergence is explained by [[AIInfrastructureInvestment]] crushing FCF while earnings (which exclude much of the capex burden) still grow.

Takeaway: when capex intensity is rising fast, P/E flatters valuation; P/FCF reveals the hit.

## The index-level read (per [[outlining-the-bull-and-bear-arguments]])
Muir extends the P/FCF argument from [[MAG7]] to the full S&P 500 in his 20 April bull/bear piece:
- **S&P 500 P/FCF is at the highest point of the last decade.**
- NTM EPS is rising, but NTM FCF is **not rising at the same pace** — the divergence is the [[AIInfrastructureInvestment]] drag writ large at the index level.
- *"The risk that CAPEX doesn't produce the expected return-on-investment is an underappreciated black swan paddling in the background."*
- **DotCom analog:** *"It's difficult to know when free-cash-flow matters. However, when it comes to the forefront, the consequences will be devastating."*
- Paired with [[EquityRiskPremium]] at its lowest since early 2025, P/FCF provides the fundamental underside of the bear case that Paulo's price-action thesis ([[AnatomyOfACrash]]) expects to collapse in stage 4.
