# Fade Geopolitical Angst

The canonical [[MarkoPapic]] / [[BCAResearch]] framework: **geopolitical events rarely move longer-term asset prices, so sell the spike and fade the fear.** The practical corollary: if you are worried about a specific event, there is usually little reason to position for it — the worry is already priced in.

This is the *rule of thumb* derived from Papic's deeper analytical framework, [[GeopoliticalAlpha]]. The rule works most of the time precisely because most events don't change **material constraints on policymakers**; the framework itself exists to identify the exceptions.

## The rule and its limits
- **Rule:** most geopolitical events follow a fast mean-reversion pattern. Positioning for the downside before or during the event almost always loses money.
- **Exception:** some events trigger a genuine *re-pricing* of the long-run outlook. Papic's own chart includes historical cases (the 1940s gold market is the canonical example) where the market took years to absorb the new regime, not days.

## Muir's structural pushback (per [[geopolitics-doesnt-change-prices-in-the-long-run]], 12 March 2026)
Six days after the Papic podcast, [[KevinMuir]] writes a short essay explicitly pushing back on the *over-application* of the fade — while leaving the framework itself intact. Via a quote from [[LastLibertarianSanFrancisco]]: *"Everyone loves to say that geopolitics doesn't move asset prices, but that's just wrong. Gold over the past four years would have something to say about that!"*

The argument: **most geopolitical events don't have lasting price impact, but some are transformative and those cannot be faded.** The 2022 Russia reserve confiscation was a canonical transformative event — gold diverged massively from its 2007–end-2022 regression against TIPS + DXY, exactly because the [[LedgerToRealWorldThesis]] had changed the structural buyer. Muir's counsel: *"Just don't make that forecast [that the Iran war will subside] because geopolitics never matters. Gold would like to disagree."*

This is consistent with, not contradictory to, Papic's framework. Both authors say: identify whether you are in the default bucket or the exception bucket, and do the analysis to know which.

## Relevance in April 2026
[[KevinMuir]] uses Papic's framework in [[outlining-the-bull-and-bear-arguments]] as a **bull argument** — "the buy the geopolitical angst trade worked in spades… it was just a little late in kicking in." The S&P closed pre-war at 6879 and closed 17 April at >7100.

But Muir immediately turns the same framework into a **bear argument** via the exception clause: the current war may belong to the "long-term re-pricing" bucket — permanent higher global military/navy spending (crowding out productive assets), financial repression, and a globalization reversal that diminishes the US premium. Muir's 2022-era [[Gold]] piece is invoked as a personal precedent where he was right on a long-term geopolitical re-pricing (Russia-asset confiscation → [[LedgerToRealWorldThesis]]) that the market took a while to absorb.

The "fade geopolitical angst" framework also connects to [[PauloMacro]]'s [[NorthStar]] via mirror image: Papic argues events are almost always faded; Paulo argues this specific event cannot be faded because Iran's Nash strategy is a structural change, not a moment.

## Papic's own 2026 exception — the January 6 long-Brent call
Per [[my-buddy-marko-broke-the-market]] (6 March 2026 podcast): Papic himself flipped long Brent on **6 January 2026** — the first time since 7 October 2023 that he stopped fading Middle East geopolitics. He identified four material-constraint changes: (1) US using the "R-word" (regime change) = Iran existentially threatened; (2) Gulf Arab plausible deniability; (3) internal Iranian regime discord in December 2025; (4) Iranian drone-technology asymmetry (Houthi precedent). This is the framework working correctly — the fade is off when material constraints have flipped.

## "Marko broke the market" — the complacency trap
[[KevinMuir]]'s thesis in [[my-buddy-marko-broke-the-market]]: Papic's framework has been so widely adopted that the market no longer prices geopolitical risk even when it should. Papic accepts the parallel to [[NormanAngell]]'s 1909 *[[TheGreatIllusion]]*. The framework's strength (correct most of the time) becomes a structural vulnerability (the one event that does change material constraints is now unhedged). Papic's self-deprecating line: *"I'm the Norman Angell of our era."*

## April 2026 vindication on equities
Ironically, the *complacency-trap* framing has not yet been validated — the framework itself has. Through 20 April:
- S&P closed 17 April at **7126 vs. pre-war 6879** — the market faded the war in spades.
- Nasdaq 13 consecutive up days; first since 15 July 2013.
- Muir's own bull-pillar #1 in [[outlining-the-bull-and-bear-arguments]] concedes: *"the buy the geopolitical angst trade worked in spades… just a little late in kicking in."*

So the six-weeks-into-the-war empirical scoreboard says: the *rule-of-thumb* fade worked on risk assets; the *exception clause* worked on oil (where Papic himself flagged the trade on 6 January). The complacency-trap thesis requires a further [[TomHanksHasCovidMoment]] / [[AnatomyOfACrash]] stage-4 collapse to be vindicated — which hasn't happened yet.
