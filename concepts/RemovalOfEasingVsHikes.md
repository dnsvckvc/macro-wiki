# Removal of Easing vs Actual Hikes

[[KevinMuir]]'s most load-bearing 2026 framework distinction, formalized in [[why-did-the-front-end-get-hammered]] (27 April 2026):

> *"Although there should be no difference between the theory of removing implied easing and the practice of raising rates, in practice, there is."* (Muir's pseudo-Yogi-Berra)
>
> *"It's one thing not to ease, but it's completely another to raise rates."*

## The asymmetry

| | **Removing implied easing** | **Actual rate hikes** |
|---|---|---|
| **What happens mechanically** | Fed declines to deliver previously-expected cuts | Fed raises target rate |
| **Market impact** | Curve repricing absorbs it | Same forward-rate move |
| **Press-conference effort** | Minimal — the Fed can frame as "data-dependent hold" | Substantial — must publicly justify tightening into a slowdown |
| **Public perception (Main Street)** | "Fed is neutral" | "Fed is fighting the people / making the recession worse" |
| **Political cost** | Low | High (especially under a Trump admin) |
| **Reversal cost** | Low (the easing was always conditional) | High (can't easily reverse a hike) |

In economics-textbook terms, both moves shift the implied policy path by the same amount. In the lived political economy of the FOMC, the second is dramatically harder to execute.

> *"Allowing the market to remove 75 basis points of implied easing is a lot different from raising 75 basis points. Standing pat with monetary policy is infinitely easier than raising rates."*

## Why it matters in April 2026

The framework explains the **breaking crude→SOFR regression** Muir documents in [[why-did-the-front-end-get-hammered]]:

1. **First leg (Feb 28 → April 11):** crude rose, market removed all 3 priced cuts and added a small hike chance. Dec 2026 SOFR went from "just under 3%" → 3.875% (~87.5bps). This was almost *entirely* in the removal-of-easing channel. Asymmetry was operative but on the easy side.

2. **Pivot (last two weeks of April):** with 3 cuts already removed, the next 75bps of forward-rate tightening would require **actual hikes**. The market is sniffing this out — yellow dots in the regression sit *below* the line; rates have sunk back to pre-FOMC levels for given crude. Translation: the market no longer believes the next leg of crude → SOFR transmission will hold 1:1.

3. **Forecast (next leg, if crude spikes):** *"If we get this scenario, just like in 2007-2008, the Federal Reserve will come up with tons of excuses why they don't need to tighten into an energy supply shock."* The trade is to *bet against* the prior crude-vs-SOFR regression. Long Brent + long Dec 2026 SOFR is the simplest expression.

## Why this is more than a rates trick

The framework provides the **reconciliation** between [[FedLookThrough]] (Fed's historical instinct to ignore energy spikes) and the actual Q1 2026 Fed (which was uncharacteristically hawkish):

- The 2026 Fed wasn't *truly* breaking from look-through. It was just *not delivering already-expected cuts* — easier than actively hiking.
- For the *next* leg of crude, the Fed cannot stay in the removal-of-easing channel because there's no easing left to remove. So look-through reasserts itself by default.
- Muir: *"The market was correct in its assessment that the Federal Reserve understands that they are powerless to fix an energy supply problem and all they can do is make it worse. It was just unfortunate that going into the war, there was so much easing priced into the curve."*

## The economy/inflation reconciliation

The framework also reconciles a tension elsewhere in the wiki:
- [[OilPriceRecessionDebate]]: Bernanke says it's the rate response that causes the recession; Hamilton says it's the oil shock itself.
- 2026 first leg: the rate response *was* the dominant transmission ([[making-sense-of-the-last-two-months]]: Dec SOFR drove SPX through 6475 [[JPMCollarTrade]] → 30 March low). Hamilton-style.
- 2026 second leg (if crude spikes again): no more rate-response runway → recession transmits *directly* through real-economy demand destruction. Bernanke-style. Same author, two different leg-by-leg verdicts.
