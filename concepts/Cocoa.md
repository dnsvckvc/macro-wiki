# Cocoa

Soft agricultural commodity; concentrated supply (Ivory Coast + Ghana = majority of world output). Used in the wiki primarily as the **counter-example** to the [[CommoditySupercycle]] thesis — a commodity where high prices *did* cure high prices in classical fashion.

## The 2,500 → 12,000 → collapse round trip ([[OleHansen]] in [[macrovoices-529-ole-hansen]])

1. **Setup (pre-spike).** Cocoa farmer prices had been depressed for years; production capacity not maintained.
2. **Catalyst.** Ivory Coast and Ghana hit by adverse weather sequence — too much rain, then too dry. Production crashed.
3. **Spike.** Price ran from ~$2,500/tonne to ~$12,000/tonne.
4. **Demand response.** Chocolate manufacturers reduced cocoa content per bar; **shrinkflation** (smaller bars at the same price); European grindings fell to **lowest level since 2013**.
5. **Supply response.** High prices reached farmers; production ramped meaningfully.
6. **Collapse.** Price round-tripped back toward the original range.
7. **Set-up for next spike.** Reduced cocoa content + reduced grindings have *cut* steady demand, but production is still high. Sets up a new under-supply spike when (a) supply slips on weather, or (b) one or both demand-destruction levers reverse.

Hansen: *"This is just a classic example of how it goes through these various big cycles where supply and demand responds to both lower prices and higher prices."*

Speculative tangent: an Israeli company is developing **lab-grown cocoa**.

## Why cocoa is the cycle counter-example

Hansen uses cocoa to make the load-bearing point that **the energy-transition [[CommoditySupercycle]] is dangerous precisely because it lacks cocoa's escape valves**:
- Cocoa demand has substitutes (less cocoa per bar, smaller bars).
- Cocoa supply responds in 1–2 growing seasons.
- Energy and base metals have *neither* — substitution at scale takes a decade, new production capacity takes 5–15 years.

So cocoa is the textbook commodity cycle. Energy/metals in a supercycle are not.
