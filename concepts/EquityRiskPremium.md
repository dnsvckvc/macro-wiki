# Equity Risk Premium (ERP)

The spread between the earnings yield of an equity index and the yield on risk-free bonds. The standard long-run valuation metric for stocks vs. fixed income. Short-run noise; **shockingly accurate for multi-year returns** (per [[KevinMuir]]).

## April 2026 status
Per [[KevinMuir]] in [[outlining-the-bull-and-bear-arguments]]: **the S&P 500 ERP has not been lower since the initial days of the Trump administration (early 2025).** Muir uses this as a pillar of the bear case: at current ERP levels, the multi-year expected return on stocks over bonds is near a generational low.

*"I know it's quaint to talk about the equity risk premium, but over the long-run, the difference between the valuation of stocks and bonds drives returns way more than investors acknowledge. Sure, over the short-run, it's useless, but for measuring returns over multiple years, it's shockingly accurate."*

## Wiki relevance
- Reinforces Muir's [[PriceToFreeCashFlow]] argument from [[what-not-to-buy]]: stocks are expensive on the metrics that matter over multi-year horizons.
- Complements [[PauloMacro]]'s crowding/positioning arguments from [[intensely-concerned-for-risk-with-a-catch]] and [[oil-has-turned-a-very-big-corner]]: low ERP + over-owned US assets = large downside asymmetry.
- The 2025-low ERP persists even after the war-era correction — the "20% MAG7 drawdown makes it cheap" narrative is addressable by P/E but not by ERP.
